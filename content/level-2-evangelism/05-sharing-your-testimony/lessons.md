# Lessons — Sharing Your Testimony

---

## Lesson 1 — Why Your Story Matters

### HOOK
Think about the stories that have shaped you most — not the ones you read in a book, but the ones someone told you from their own life. A friend describing how they got through a loss, a parent sharing how they met your other parent, a stranger telling you how they found hope when everything fell apart. Personal stories land differently than facts. They carry weight because they are real, and reality is hard to argue with.

### CORE TEACHING
Your testimony — the story of how you came to know Jesus Christ — is one of the most powerful tools God has given you for evangelism. It is not a sermon, and it does not require a theology degree to deliver. It is simply your account of what God has done in your life, and no one can dispute it. When you tell someone what your life was like before Christ, how you encountered Him, and how He has changed you, you are doing something far greater than sharing an experience. You are demonstrating the power of the gospel in real time. The book of Revelation reveals something remarkable about the battle between good and evil. It says that believers overcome the accuser by the blood of the Lamb and by the word of their testimony. Your story is not just a nice encouragement — it is a weapon. Every time you share what God has done, you push back against the lies of the enemy and shine light into someone's darkness. But there is a danger here. You can tell your story in a way that makes people admire you, or you can tell it in a way that makes them hunger for Jesus. The difference is whether you keep yourself at the centre or point relentlessly to Him. Think of it this way: if someone showed you a photograph of a beautiful sunset, you would not spend the whole conversation talking about the camera. The camera is just the tool. The sunset is the point. Your life is the camera. Jesus is the sunset. Your testimony must never become a showcase for your transformation but always a window through which others see the One who transformed you. When you share your story with humility and honesty, you give the person listening something they cannot反驳: a real encounter with a real God. And that opens a door for the Holy Spirit to do what only He can do — convince and convert.

### KEY SCRIPTURES
- **Revelation 12:11** — "And they overcame him by the blood of the Lamb and by the word of their testimony, and they did not love their lives to the death." (NKJV) [VERIFY]
- **1 Peter 3:15** — "But sanctify the Lord God in your hearts, and always be ready to give a defense to everyone who asks you a reason for the hope that is in you, with meekness and fear." (NKJV) [VERIFY]
- **Acts 1:8** — "But you shall receive power when the Holy Spirit has come upon you; and you shall be witnesses to Me in Jerusalem, and in all Judea and Samaria, and to the end of the earth." (NKJV) [VERIFY]

### PRACTICAL APPLICATION
This week, write a rough first draft of your testimony in three short paragraphs — before Christ, how you came to Christ, and after Christ. Do not worry about making it perfect. Just get it on paper.

### SUMMARY
- Your testimony is powerful because it is your real, personal experience of God's grace.
- The purpose of your story is not to glorify you but to point others to Jesus.
- Revelation 12:11 tells us that our testimony is part of how we overcome spiritual opposition.

## Lesson 2 — Before Christ: Honest Without Glorifying

### HOOK
Everyone alive has a story about what they were running from, searching for, or hiding behind before they found what they were really looking for. Maybe it was approval from people who never gave it. Maybe it was a string of relationships that always ended the same way. Maybe it was simply a quiet, nagging sense that something was wrong and you did not know what. Before Christ, every one of us was lost — we just expressed it differently.

### CORE TEACHING
The first section of your testimony describes your life before you knew Jesus. This part matters because it establishes the problem that only God could solve. But how you tell it makes all the difference. Some believers make the mistake of spending too much time here, describing their past sins in vivid detail. This does two things wrong: it can make sin sound entertaining, and it puts the spotlight on you rather than on the grace that rescued you. The goal is not to shock people with how bad you were. The goal is to help them see that you needed a Saviour — just like they do. Scripture says that all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God. Whether your story involves visible rebellion or quiet religious self-righteousness, the underlying condition was the same: you were separated from God and unable to fix it. Be honest about your past but keep it brief. A few clear sentences are enough. You do not need to catalogue every sin; you just need to paint an accurate picture of your spiritual condition. The Apostle Paul, in his testimony before King Agrippa, mentioned his religious background and then his persecution of Christians. He did not linger. He stated the facts and moved to the turning point. Follow his example. The purpose of the "before Christ" section is to create contrast. The darker the background, the brighter the light shines. But the light — not the darkness — must remain the focus. If someone walks away from your testimony remembering more about your sin than about your Saviour, you have told it backwards. Let your past simply be the canvas on which God's grace is displayed.

### KEY SCRIPTURES
- **Romans 3:23** — "For all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God." (NKJV) [VERIFY]
- **Ephesians 2:1-3** — "And you He made alive, who were dead in trespasses and sins, in which you once walked according to the course of this world, according to the prince of the power of the air, the spirit who now works in the sons of disobedience." (NKJV) [VERIFY]
- **Acts 26:4-5** — "My manner of life from my youth, which was spent from the beginning among my own nation at Jerusalem, all the Jews know. They knew me from the first, if they were willing to testify, that according to the strictest sect of our religion I lived a Pharisee." (NKJV) [VERIFY]

### PRACTICAL APPLICATION
Write down no more than three sentences describing your life before Christ. Focus on your spiritual condition — what you were searching for, what you trusted in, or what you were running from. Keep it honest but brief.

### SUMMARY
- The "before Christ" section establishes your need for a Saviour without glorifying sin.
- Keep this part short — a few clear sentences are enough to show the contrast.
- Paul's testimony in Acts 26 models honesty without lingering on the past.

## Lesson 3 — The Turning Point: How You Met Christ

### HOOK
Every good story has a moment where everything changes — the scene where the door opens, the decision is made, the direction shifts. For some, that moment came in the middle of a crisis. For others, it came quietly, almost without drama. But in every true conversion, something happened that was not just human. God broke in.

### CORE TEACHING
The turning point of your testimony is the moment you repented of your sin and placed your faith in Jesus Christ. This is the heart of your story, and it must be told with care because it carries the most weight. The danger here is subtle: you can accidentally make your decision the hero of the story rather than the God who drew you to Himself. Scripture is clear that no one comes to Christ unless the Father draws them. Your conversion was not primarily your decision — it was God's work in you. Ezekiel 36 describes God saying, "I will give you a new heart and put a new spirit within you." That is what happened when you were saved. God took out your heart of stone and gave you a heart of flesh. Your testimony should reflect this. You did not find God; He found you. You did not decide to follow Jesus on your own; the Holy Spirit opened your eyes and gave you the desire to repent and believe. Describe what led up to that moment — perhaps a conversation, a sermon, a Scripture passage, a personal crisis — but keep the focus on what God did in that moment. Paul's testimony on the road to Damascus is the classic example. He was not searching for Jesus. He was hunting Christians. But Jesus stopped him, spoke to him, and changed everything. Paul's testimony does not celebrate his own openness; it celebrates Christ's sovereign intervention. Your testimony should do the same. Whether your conversion was dramatic or quiet, the miracle is identical: a dead sinner was made alive. That is what people need to hear. Be specific about the circumstances if they help the story, but always direct the attention to the One who acted.

### KEY SCRIPTURES
- **Ezekiel 36:26** — "I will give you a new heart and put a new spirit within you; I will take the heart of stone out of your flesh and give you a heart of flesh." (NKJV) [VERIFY]
- **2 Corinthians 5:17** — "Therefore, if anyone is in Christ, he is a new creation; old things have passed away; behold, all things have become new." (NKJV) [VERIFY]
- **Acts 26:12-18** — Paul's account of his conversion on the road to Damascus. (NKJV) [VERIFY]
- **John 6:44** — "No one can come to Me unless the Father who sent Me draws him; and I will raise him up at the last day." (NKJV) [VERIFY]

### PRACTICAL APPLICATION
Write three to four sentences describing exactly how you came to Christ. Include the circumstances, what you understood about the gospel, and what you did in response. Then read it aloud and make sure Christ is the centre, not your decision.

### SUMMARY
- The turning point is where you repented and believed in Jesus — the miracle of new birth.
- Keep the focus on what God did, not on your decision or sincerity.
- Ezekiel 36:26 and 2 Corinthians 5:17 show that conversion is a divine act, not human self-improvement.

## Lesson 4 — After Christ: The Evidence of New Life

### HOOK
Anyone can say they have changed. But change that lasts, change that is visible, change that other people notice — that is harder to fake. When someone tells you they met Jesus, the most compelling evidence is not the story of the meeting. It is the life that has been different ever since.

### CORE TEACHING
The third movement of your testimony describes what your life has looked like since you came to Christ. This section is crucial because it answers the question every listener is silently asking: "Did it actually work?" You are not required to claim perfection. In fact, claiming perfection would be dishonest and would set an impossible standard. But you should be able to point to real, observable changes that God has made in your life. Maybe He gave you peace where there was constant anxiety. Maybe He changed your attitude toward someone you could not forgive. Maybe He gave you a desire to read Scripture when before you had no interest. These changes do not need to be dramatic to be powerful. The Bible says that if anyone is in Christ, he is a new creation. That means the old things have passed away and new things have come. Your testimony should reflect that reality. However, you must be careful not to present these changes as your own achievement. It is easy to sound like you are listing your spiritual accomplishments — look how much I have grown, look how different I am now. That is pride dressed up as a testimony. Instead, present the changes as evidence of God's ongoing work. You are not the potter; you are the clay. The changes in your life are the marks of His hands. When you describe how God has changed you, you are doing more than telling your story. You are giving a living illustration of the gospel. You are showing that Jesus does not just forgive — He transforms. And that transformation is one of the most compelling arguments for the truth of Christianity that exists.

### KEY SCRIPTURES
- **2 Corinthians 5:17** — "Therefore, if anyone is in Christ, he is a new creation; old things have passed away; behold, all things have become new." (NKJV) [VERIFY]
- **Ezekiel 36:27** — "I will put My Spirit within you and cause you to walk in My statutes, and you will keep My judgments and do them." (NKJV) [VERIFY]
- **Ephesians 2:4-5** — "But God, who is rich in mercy, because of His great love with which He loved us, even when we were dead in trespasses, made us alive together with Christ (by grace you have been saved)." (NKJV) [VERIFY]
- **Philippians 1:6** — "Being confident of this very thing, that He who has begun a good work in you will complete it until the day of Jesus Christ." (NKJV) [VERIFY]

### PRACTICAL APPLICATION
List three specific changes Christ has made in your life since you came to know Him. These can be internal (attitudes, desires, peace) or external (habits, relationships, priorities). Keep each one to one sentence.

### SUMMARY
- The "after Christ" section shows that the gospel actually changes lives, not just beliefs.
- Be honest about ongoing struggles while celebrating real transformation.
- Present the changes as God's work, not your own spiritual resume.

## Lesson 5 — Bridging Your Testimony to the Gospel

### HOOK
A beautiful story that leads nowhere is just entertainment. You can tell someone about the most amazing meal you ever ate, but if you never tell them how to get to the restaurant, they will stay hungry. Your testimony is like that. It can move people, inspire them, even make them cry — but if it does not lead them to the cross, it has not done its job.

### CORE TEACHING
Your testimony is not the gospel. It is a bridge to the gospel. This distinction is critical. Your personal experience of God's grace is powerful, but it does not save anyone. Salvation comes through the death and resurrection of Jesus Christ, and your testimony must deliberately point people to that finished work. The danger is that someone hears your story, feels inspired, and walks away without ever understanding that they need to repent of their sin and trust in Christ. They appreciated your journey but never made their own decision. To prevent this, every testimony needs a gospel bridge — a moment where you transition from "what God did for me" to "what God did for the whole world through Jesus." This is where you bring in the cross. You say something like: "The same Jesus who changed my life died on the cross for your sins and rose again. My story is just one example of what He can do for anyone who turns to Him." That shift turns your personal story into an invitation. Paul modelled this perfectly in Acts 26. After describing his conversion, he immediately connected it to the death and resurrection of Christ and then made a direct appeal to King Agrippa. He did not leave the king to admire his story from a distance. He pushed toward a decision. Your testimony must do the same. Write a gospel bridge — one or two sentences that connect your personal experience to the universal truth of Christ's sacrifice and resurrection. Practice saying it until it feels natural. This bridge is what turns your testimony from a story about you into an opportunity for someone else to meet Jesus.

### KEY SCRIPTURES
- **1 Corinthians 15:3-4** — "For I delivered to you first of all that which I also received: that Christ died for our sins according to the Scriptures, and that He was buried, and that He rose again the third day according to the Scriptures." (NKJV) [VERIFY]
- **Acts 26:22-23** — "Therefore, having obtained help from God, to this day I stand, witnessing both to small and great, saying no other things than those which the prophets and Moses said would come — that the Christ would suffer, that He would be the first to rise from the dead, and would proclaim light to the Jewish people and to the Gentiles." (NKJV) [VERIFY]
- **Romans 10:9** — "That if you confess with your mouth the Lord Jesus and believe in your heart that God has raised Him from the dead, you will be saved." (NKJV) [VERIFY]

### PRACTICAL APPLICATION
Write a one-sentence gospel bridge that connects your personal story to the death and resurrection of Jesus. Memorise it so you can add it naturally every time you share your testimony.

### SUMMARY
- Your testimony is a bridge, not the destination — it must lead to the cross and resurrection.
- The gospel bridge transitions from your personal experience to Christ's finished work.
- Paul's testimony in Acts 26 shows that every testimony should end with an invitation to respond.

## Lesson 6 — Brevity, Clarity, and Humility

### HOOK
Nobody has ever complained that a testimony was too short, but many have sat through one that was too long. Attention spans are short, opportunities are unpredictable, and the person you are talking to may only give you a few minutes. The question is not whether you can tell your whole story. The question is whether you can tell enough of it to make them want to hear more.

### CORE TEACHING
A well-prepared testimony should take no more than three to five minutes to deliver. That is roughly the length of a single song. If you cannot share the essential parts of your story in that window, you have not done the hard work of editing. Brevity is not about rushing. It is about choosing what matters most and cutting everything else. What matters most in your testimony is your spiritual condition before Christ, how you came to faith, and the difference Christ has made. Everything else — interesting details, emotional backstory, supporting characters — should serve those three things or be removed. Clarity is equally important. Avoid religious language that your listener may not understand. Do not say "I was convicted of my sin" if you can say "I realised for the first time that I had done wrong against God." Do not say "I was justified by faith" if you can say "God declared me right with Him because I trusted in Jesus." Use words that anyone can understand. The goal is not to sound educated; it is to be understood. And then there is humility. Your testimony should never sound like you are proud of your past or proud of your present. You were lost and God found you. You were broken and God healed you. You were dead and God made you alive. The tone should be one of gratitude, not accomplishment. Colossians 4:6 instructs believers to let their speech always be with grace, seasoned with salt — attractive, not off-putting. If your testimony comes across as superior, people will tune out. If it comes across as grateful and real, they will lean in. Practice your testimony out loud. Time yourself. Cut it down. Refine your words until the clearest, shortest version of your story is also the most powerful version.

### KEY SCRIPTURES
- **Colossians 4:6** — "Let your speech always be with grace, seasoned with salt, that you may know how you ought to answer each one." (NKJV) [VERIFY]
- **Proverbs 27:2** — "Let another man praise you, and not your own mouth; a stranger, and not your own lips." (NKJV) [VERIFY]
- **Ecclesiastes 5:2** — "Do not be rash with your mouth, and let not your heart utter anything hastily before God. For God is in heaven, and you on earth; therefore let your words be few." (NKJV) [VERIFY]

### PRACTICAL APPLICATION
Time yourself sharing your full testimony out loud. If it takes longer than five minutes, cut it down. Keep refining until you can share the core of your story in three to five minutes with natural, humble words.

### SUMMARY
- Keep your testimony to three to five minutes — brevity honours your listener's time and attention.
- Use simple, clear language that anyone can understand, not religious jargon.
- Humility means telling your story with gratitude, not pride — God did the work, not you.

## Lesson 7 — From Your Story to Theirs

### HOOK
Imagine handing someone a map and then walking away before they can ask where they are standing. That is what it feels like when you share your testimony and never give the other person a chance to respond. Your story is not a monologue. It is the first half of a conversation that could change someone's eternity.

### CORE TEACHING
The final element of every testimony is the transition from your story to the other person's story. Paul modelled this perfectly in Acts 26. After sharing his testimony, he looked at King Agrippa and asked a direct question: "King Agrippa, do you believe the prophets? I know that you do believe." He turned his personal account into a personal challenge. Your testimony should do the same. After you share what God has done for you, the natural next step is to ask a question that invites the other person to consider their own standing with God. You might ask, "Has anything like that ever happened to you?" or "What is your understanding of who Jesus is?" or simply, "Does any of this make sense to you?" The question does not need to be confrontational. It just needs to open a door. The goal is not to pressure someone into a decision on the spot. The goal is to move from telling to listening, from presenting to engaging. Once you ask the question, your job shifts. Now you listen. You listen to their story, their doubts, their questions, their objections. And as you listen, you look for ways to connect what they are saying back to the gospel. This is where evangelism becomes relational rather than transactional. First Peter 3:15-16 reminds us to share our hope with gentleness and respect, keeping a good conscience so that those who speak against us are put to shame. That gentleness is most visible in how we handle the conversation after we have spoken. We are not trying to win an argument. We are trying to win a person. Your testimony opens the door, but the Holy Spirit does the work. Your job is to be faithful, clear, and loving — and then to trust God with the result.

### KEY SCRIPTURES
- **Acts 26:27-29** — "King Agrippa, do you believe the prophets? I know that you do believe." Then Agrippa said to Paul, "You almost persuade me to become a Christian." And Paul said, "I would to God that not only you, but also all who hear me today, might become both almost and altogether such as I am, except for these chains." (NKJV) [VERIFY]
- **1 Peter 3:15-16** — "But sanctify the Lord God in your hearts, and always be ready to give a defense to everyone who asks you a reason for the hope that is in you, with meekness and fear; having a good conscience, that when they defame you as evildoers, those who revile your good conduct in Christ may be ashamed." (NKJV) [VERIFY]
- **2 Timothy 2:24-25** — "And a servant of the Lord must not quarrel but be gentle to all, able to teach, patient, in humility correcting those who are in opposition, if God perhaps will grant them repentance, so that they may know the truth." (NKJV) [VERIFY]

### PRACTICAL APPLICATION
This week, share your testimony with one person — a friend, colleague, neighbour, or family member — and after you finish, ask them a simple question about their own spiritual journey. Listen more than you speak.

### SUMMARY
- Your testimony should always end with a question that invites the other person to respond.
- After you share, shift from telling to listening — let the conversation become about them.
- Paul's example in Acts 26 shows that personal appeal is the natural conclusion of every testimony.
