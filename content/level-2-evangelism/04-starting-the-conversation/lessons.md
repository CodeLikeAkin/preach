# Lessons: Starting the Conversation

---

## Lesson 1: The Fear of the First Sentence

### Hook
You are standing next to a stranger — in a queue, at a bus stop, beside a desk at work. You know you should say something about what matters most. Your mouth goes dry. Your mind goes blank. The seconds tick by, and the moment passes. Most believers know this feeling, and it is the single greatest barrier to sharing the gospel. The fear of the first sentence has silenced more Christians than persecution ever could.

### Core Teaching
The fear of the first sentence is real, and pretending it does not exist will not help anyone overcome it. But the answer is not to wait until the fear goes away, because it will not. The answer is to act in spite of it. Scripture tells us that God has not given us a spirit of fear, but of power, of love, and of a sound mind. This means fear does not come from Him. It comes from our own weakness, from the enemy's accusations, and from a misplaced concern about what others think of us. The fear of man brings a snare, but the one who fears the Lord has nothing ultimate to fear from man. Consider what is actually at stake. The worst that can happen to you in a conversation is a moment of awkwardness or a polite refusal. The person you are speaking to will likely forget your awkward opening within minutes. But the best that can happen is eternal — a soul confronted with the truth of Christ, a heart softened by the Spirit, a life rescued from darkness. The fear of the first sentence shrinks when you compare the temporary discomfort of a few seconds against the eternal weight of what is being offered. One practical way to break through this fear is to practice. The first time you say something spiritual to a stranger, it feels terrifying. The tenth time, it feels uncomfortable. The hundredth time, it begins to feel natural. Your brain learns that nothing catastrophic happens when you open your mouth. No lightning strikes. No one screams at you. Most people are surprisingly open to talking about deep things when approached with genuine kindness. The cross of Christ was not a comfortable event, and the resurrection was not announced in a boardroom. The gospel came into the world through sacrifice and power, not through comfort and safety. If the Son of God could face the cross for the sake of souls, surely we can face a few seconds of discomfort for the same cause.

### Key Scriptures
- 2 Timothy 1:7 — "For God has not given us a spirit of fear, but of power and of love and of a sound mind." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Proverbs 29:25 — "The fear of man brings a snare, but whoever trusts in the LORD shall be safe." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Acts 4:29 — "Now, Lord, look on their threats, and grant to Your servants that with all boldness they may speak Your word." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Ephesians 6:19-20 — "that I may open my mouth boldly to make known the mystery of the gospel, for which I am an ambassador in chains; that in it I may speak boldly, as I ought to speak." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]

### Practical Application
This week, choose one stranger you will speak to each day — a cashier, a person in line, a neighbor — and say a simple greeting followed by one genuine question. That is all. No spiritual content required. Do this for seven days. On day eight, add one spiritual element: ask someone, "Do you ever think about what happens after this life?" Write down how you felt before and after each attempt. Notice how the fear decreases with repetition.

### Summary
- The fear of the first sentence is the biggest barrier to evangelism, but it can be overcome through practice and by keeping eternal stakes in view.
- God has given us a spirit of power and love, not fear — the fear we feel does not come from Him.
- Acting in spite of fear, even with small steps, retrains our minds and builds lasting confidence.

## Lesson 2: Simple Openers

### Hook
Imagine you are at a bus stop and the person beside you is wearing a wristwatch. You could talk about the weather — or you could say, "That is a well-designed watch. It is amazing how something so small can have so much complexity. Do you ever think about who designed all of this?" In one sentence, you have moved from an observation to an open door. The simplest openers are often the most effective.

### Core Teaching
The best opening lines are not clever or complicated. They are natural extensions of the moment you are already in. The woman at the well did not expect a theology lecture from a thirsty Jewish traveler, yet Jesus began with something as ordinary as, "Give Me a drink." He used what was right in front of Him — a well, a woman with a water pot, the heat of the day — and turned it into a conversation about living water. You can do the same with almost anything. A news headline about a tragedy can lead to, "Does that kind of thing ever make you think about what really matters?" A comment about someone's child can lead to, "Being a parent really makes you think about the bigger questions, does not it?" A remark about a beautiful sunset can lead to, "It makes you wonder if there is something more behind all this, does not it?" The key is not to force it but to genuinely connect the observation to the question. People can tell when you are using a technique versus when you are genuinely interested in them. The best openers come from a heart of love, not from a memorized script. That said, preparation is not the enemy of sincerity. Having a few simple questions ready in your mind means you will not freeze when the moment comes. Some believers carry a gospel tract as a conversation starter. Others use a question they have practiced until it feels natural: "Do you have a spiritual background?" or "What do you think about God?" These are not manipulative — they are honest invitations. The goal is not to trap someone but to open a door. Once the door is open, you do not need to rush through it. A conversation is not a race. The cross and resurrection give us something worth discussing, but the person across from you needs to know you care about them before they will care about what you believe.

### Key Scriptures
- John 4:7-10 — "A woman of Samaria came to draw water. Jesus said to her, 'Give Me a drink.'... If you knew the gift of God, and who it is who says to you, 'Give Me a drink,' you would have asked Him, and He would have given you living water." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Colossians 4:5-6 — "Walk in wisdom toward those who are outside, redeeming the time. Let your speech always be with grace, seasoned with salt, that you may know how you ought to answer each one." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]

### Practical Application
This week, prepare three natural conversation openers that fit your context. Write them down. Practice saying them aloud until they feel comfortable. For example: (1) "That is a beautiful sunset — do you ever wonder if there is a Creator behind all this?" (2) "You mentioned your grandmother — did she have a faith that shaped her life?" (3) "I was reading about current events and it made me think about where the world is heading. Do you ever think about that?" Use at least one of these three openers in a real conversation this week. Afterward, write down what happened and what you learned.

### Summary
- The best openers are natural observations that lead to spiritual questions, not forced transitions from unrelated topics.
- Preparation and practice reduce the fear of the first sentence without making your approach insincere.
- Jesus modeled using ordinary moments as bridges to eternal truths, and we can follow His example.

## Lesson 3: Asking Good Questions

### Hook
Imagine walking into a doctor's office and having the physician immediately hand you a prescription without asking a single question about your symptoms. You would walk out. A good doctor listens before treating. In the same way, a good evangelist asks questions before giving answers. The person you are speaking to has a story, a set of beliefs, and perhaps wounds you know nothing about. Questions are the key that unlocks all of it.

### Core Teaching
Questions do more than gather information. They show respect. When you ask someone what they believe and why, you are telling them that their thoughts matter. You are not treating them as a project but as a person. This is the heart of the gospel — God did not treat us as projects but as people He loved enough to send His Son. Asking good questions also buys you time to understand where the person is coming from. You cannot answer an objection you have not heard, and you cannot address a need you have not identified. A well-placed question like, "What do you think happens after we die?" is diagnostic. The answer tells you whether this person believes in an afterlife, whether they have thought about eternity at all, and whether they are open to discussing it. Another powerful question is, "Do you have a spiritual background?" This is non-threatening. It invites the person to tell you about their religious upbringing without feeling defensive. Most people enjoy talking about themselves, so this question often leads to a relaxed and open conversation. The question, "What do you think God is like?" reveals a person's theology. Someone who says, "God is love, and He would never judge anyone," has a very different worldview from someone who says, "I do not think God exists at all." Each answer tells you where to go next. Jesus was a master of questions. He asked the Samaritan woman for water. He asked the blind man what he wanted. He asked Peter who people said He was. He asked the rich young ruler about the commandments. Questions put the ball in the other person's court and give the Holy Spirit room to work on their conscience. Your goal is not to win a debate but to create a space where the truth can be heard.

### Key Scriptures
- Proverbs 20:5 — "Counsel in the heart of man is like deep water, but a man of understanding will draw it out." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- John 4:7-26 — Jesus asked the Samaritan woman questions that led her to confront her own sin and recognize Him as the Messiah. [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Mark 10:17-22 — Jesus asked the rich young ruler about the commandments, allowing the man's own answers to reveal the idol in his heart.
- Matthew 16:13-15 — "He said to them, 'But who do you say that I am?'" — the most important question ever asked. [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]

### Practical Application
This week, memorize two diagnostic questions: (1) "What do you think happens after we die?" and (2) "Do you have a spiritual background?" Use each question at least once in a conversation with someone you do not normally talk to about spiritual things. After each conversation, write down the person's answer and one follow-up question you could have asked. Practice this until asking these questions feels as natural as asking someone about their weekend.

### Summary
- Good questions show respect, diagnose a person's worldview, and open the door for deeper conversation.
- Jesus modeled the power of asking the right question at the right time rather than lecturing.
- A well-chosen question gives the Holy Spirit room to work on a person's heart through their own answers.

## Lesson 4: The Art of Listening

### Hook
Two people sit across from each other. One talks; the other waits for their turn to talk while nodding. That is not listening — that is a pause between monologues. Real listening is rare, and in a world where everyone is fighting to be heard, the person who genuinely listens stands out like a light in darkness. Listening may be the most underrated skill in evangelism.

### Core Teaching
Listening is not passive. It is an act of love and self-control. When you listen well, you are telling the other person, "You matter. Your thoughts matter. Your struggles matter." This reflects the heart of God, who says, "Before they call, I will answer; and while they are still speaking, I will hear." There is a reason James instructs believers to be swift to hear and slow to speak. The tongue gets us into trouble, but the ear opens doors. When you listen carefully, you learn what a person actually believes — not what you assume they believe. You hear their doubts, their wounds, their objections, and sometimes their unspoken longing for something more. A person who feels heard will often say things they would never say to someone who is clearly waiting to argue. Listening also buys you the right to be heard. When someone knows you have genuinely listened to them, they are far more likely to listen to you in return. This is not manipulation — it is respect. You are giving what you hope to receive. There are practical elements to good listening. Maintain eye contact. Do not interrupt. Ask follow-up questions that show you were paying attention: "That is interesting — why do you believe that?" or "You mentioned you grew up in church — what made you step away?" These questions communicate that you value the person, not just the opportunity to share your own views. Sometimes the most powerful thing you can say is nothing at all. After someone shares something painful or honest, a moment of silence can be more powerful than a rushed answer. Silence gives the Holy Spirit room to work. It gives the person space to think. It communicates that what they said mattered enough to pause over. The cross was not a quick transaction — it was an agonizing sacrifice that took hours. The resurrection was not announced in a hurry — it was discovered slowly, piece by piece, by weeping women and doubting disciples. Eternal matters deserve the time it takes to truly hear one another.

### Key Scriptures
- James 1:19 — "So then, my beloved brethren, let every man be swift to hear, slow to speak, slow to wrath." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Proverbs 18:13 — "He who answers a matter before he hears it, it is folly and shame to him." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Proverbs 18:17 — "The first one to plead his cause seems right, until his neighbor comes and examines him." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]

### Practical Application
This week, have one conversation of at least ten minutes where your only goal is to listen. Ask a friend, coworker, or family member about their life, their struggles, or their beliefs. Do not share your own views at all. Simply ask questions and listen. At the end, thank them for sharing. Then reflect: what did you learn about them that you would not have known if you had been talking? Next, repeat this with someone you would normally try to evangelize — but still do not share the gospel. Just listen. See how the dynamic changes.

### Summary
- Listening is an act of love that communicates value and earns the right to be heard in return.
- Good listening reveals what a person actually believes, which is essential for any meaningful spiritual conversation.
- Silence and thoughtful follow-up questions give the Holy Spirit room to work in both speakers.

## Lesson 5: Natural Transitions

### Hook
A door that is locked will not open if you throw your shoulder against it, but a door that is already slightly ajar will swing open with a gentle push. The same is true in conversations. Some topics are natural hinges — death, purpose, justice, fear, gratitude, change, love, loss — and when a person mentions one of these, the door to spiritual conversation is already cracked open. The skill is learning to recognize it and push gently.

### Core Teaching
Transitions do not have to be awkward, and they should never feel like a sharp turn. The most natural transitions come from something the other person has already said. If someone mentions the death of a relative, you can say, "That must have been hard. Has it made you think about what happens after we die?" If someone complains about injustice in the world, you can say, "It really bothers me too. Do you think there will ever be a day when everything is made right?" If someone expresses gratitude for their children, you can say, "It is amazing, is not it? Do you ever wonder if there is a Giver behind all these gifts?" Each of these transitions is simply following the trail the other person has already laid. You are not changing the subject — you are going deeper into the subject they brought up. The conscience is another powerful point of transition. Every human being has an innate sense of right and wrong, and when a person talks about guilt, regret, or the desire to be a better person, you have a natural opening. You can say, "I know what you mean. It is interesting how we all have this sense that we should be better than we are. Do you think that points to something?" This engages the conscience rather than the intellect, and the conscience is a place of harmony rather than conflict. Even ordinary objects can be transition points. A watch or a phone can lead to a question about design and a Designer. A beautiful landscape can lead to a question about the Creator. A child's innocent question can lead to a reflection on the meaning of life. The key is to be observant and prayerful. Ask the Spirit to open your eyes to the hinges in everyday conversation. The cross and resurrection are the ultimate answers, but they only make sense if the person first feels the weight of the questions. Your role is not to supply answers to questions no one is asking. Your role is to help them feel the question so that the answer becomes beautiful.

### Key Scriptures
- Romans 1:20 — "For since the creation of the world His invisible attributes are clearly seen, being understood by the things that are made, even His eternal power and Godhead." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Romans 2:14-15 — "who show the work of the law written in their hearts, their conscience also bearing witness." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Ecclesiastes 3:11 — "He has made everything beautiful in its time. Also He has put eternity in their hearts." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]

### Practical Application
This week, carry a small notebook or use your phone and write down every time someone in a normal conversation mentions death, purpose, justice, morality, gratitude, fear, or eternity. At the end of each day, review your list and write one possible spiritual transition for each item. On Saturday, pick one of those transitions and try it in a real conversation. Do not aim for a full gospel presentation — just aim to move the conversation one step closer to spiritual things.

### Summary
- The best transitions follow the trail the other person has already laid, turning their own words into bridges to eternal topics.
- The conscience, everyday objects, and universal human experiences all serve as natural hinges for spiritual conversation.
- Asking the Spirit for open eyes and a prayerful heart is more important than mastering any technique.

## Lesson 6: The WDJD Framework

### Hook
Imagine walking through a dark forest without a map or a path. You would quickly become disoriented, circle back on yourself, and eventually give up. Now imagine having four simple markers that guide you from start to finish, no matter how dense the trees become. The WDJD framework is exactly that — four simple questions that keep a gospel conversation moving forward and prevent you from getting lost in arguments or tangents.

### Core Teaching
The WDJD framework is not a script to be recited word for word. It is a set of guideposts that help you know where you are in a conversation and where to go next. The letters stand for four stages that move the conversation from the person's self-assessment to their eternal destination. The first stage is about goodness. You ask a simple question: "Would you consider yourself to be a good person?" Most people will say yes, because most people measure themselves against other people rather than against God's standard. This is not a trick — it is a starting point. Their answer tells you whether they are relying on their own goodness for salvation or whether they already understand their need. The second stage deals with the law. You ask: "Do you think you have kept the Ten Commandments?" Here you move from their general self-assessment to specific moral questions. Have they lied? Have they stolen anything? Have they used God's name carelessly? Have they hated anyone? Have they looked with lust? These questions are not meant to humiliate but to bring the person face to face with God's standard. The law is a mirror that reveals the dirt — it does not clean it, but it shows why cleaning is necessary. The third stage moves to judgment. You ask: "If God judges you by that standard, do you think you would be innocent or guilty?" Most people will admit they would be guilty. This is a crucial moment because the person is now agreeing with God's assessment of their condition. The Spirit uses the law to bring conviction, and conviction prepares the heart for the gospel. The fourth stage asks about destiny: "If you stood before God today, do you think you would go to heaven or hell?" This is the question that brings the conversation to its natural climax. If the person says hell, you can ask, "Does that concern you?" A concerned heart is a ready heart. From here, the gospel — the good news of Christ's death and resurrection — becomes the most beautiful message they could possibly hear. The WDJD framework is simple enough for any believer to remember, yet deep enough to guide a meaningful conversation. It keeps you from getting lost in tangents about evolution, church scandals, or philosophical objections because you can always return to the next marker.

### Key Scriptures
- Proverbs 20:9 — "Who can say, 'I have made my heart clean, I am pure from my sin'?" [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Romans 3:19-20 — "that every mouth may be stopped, and all the world may become guilty before God. Therefore by the deeds of the law no flesh will be justified in His sight, for by the law is the knowledge of sin." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Matthew 5:27-28 — "You have heard that it was said to those of old, 'You shall not commit adultery.' But I say to you that whoever looks at a woman to lust for her has already committed adultery with her in his heart." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Romans 6:23 — "For the wages of sin is death, but the gift of God is eternal life in Christ Jesus our Lord." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]

### Practical Application
This week, write the letters WDJD on a small card: W — Would you consider yourself a good person? D — Do you think you have kept the commandments? J — Judgment — would you be innocent or guilty? D — Destiny — heaven or hell? Carry this card with you everywhere. Practice saying each question aloud five times a day until the sequence feels natural. By the end of the week, use the framework with one person. Do not worry about doing it perfectly. Even a rough attempt is better than silence.

### Summary
- The WDJD framework provides four simple guideposts: goodness, the law, judgment, and destiny, that keep a gospel conversation on track.
- The law serves as a mirror that reveals sin and prepares the heart for the good news of Christ's sacrifice.
- Practice makes the framework feel natural so that you can focus on the person rather than on remembering the next step.

## Lesson 7: Love Overcomes Fear

### Hook
A parent will run into a burning building for a child without a second thought. A friend will stay up all night with a friend in crisis without checking the clock. Love has a way of making fear feel small. In evangelism, love is not a warm feeling — it is the only force strong enough to overcome the fear of man, the fear of rejection, and the fear of awkwardness. Without love, technique is empty. With love, even imperfect words carry weight.

### Core Teaching
The greatest motivation for starting spiritual conversations is not obedience, though obedience matters. It is not guilt, though guilt can move us temporarily. It is love — love for God and love for the people He created. When you truly love someone, you want what is best for them, and nothing is better for a person than to know Christ. Think about what the alternative means. Every person who dies without Christ faces an eternity separated from God. This is not a comfortable truth, but it is a biblical one, and it gives evangelism its urgency. A person who believes that hell is real and that people are heading there will not find starting conversations optional. They will find it necessary. But urgency does not mean rudeness. Love does not bulldoze — it persuades. Love does not manipulate — it invites. Love does not threaten — it pleads. The apostle Paul wrote that the love of Christ compels us, meaning it presses us from within, it constrains us, it leaves us no choice but to speak. This is the healthiest motivation for evangelism because it focuses on Christ's love for us rather than on our own performance. We speak because we have been spoken to. We love because we have been loved first. The cross is the ultimate demonstration of this love. Christ did not wait for us to come to Him — He came to us. He did not wait for us to be ready — He made a way while we were still enemies. This is the pattern for our conversations. We do not wait for the perfect moment or the perfect person. We go. We speak. We trust the Spirit to do what we cannot. At the same time, love frees us from the burden of results. We are not responsible for whether the person responds. We are only responsible for whether we spoke. The sower sows the seed; God gives the growth. A farmer does not dig up the seeds every morning to check if they have sprouted. He sows and trusts the soil and the sun and the rain. In the same way, we sow conversations and trust the Spirit to bring the increase. Some seeds fall on hard ground. Some fall among thorns. But some fall on good soil and produce a harvest that will last forever.

### Key Scriptures
- 2 Corinthians 5:14-15 — "For the love of Christ compels us, because we judge thus: that if One died for all, then all died; and He died for all, that those who live should live no longer for themselves, but for Him who died for them and rose again." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- 1 John 4:19 — "We love Him because He first loved us." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Romans 5:8 — "But God demonstrates His own love toward us, in that while we were still sinners, Christ died for us." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- 1 Corinthians 3:6-7 — "I planted, Apollos watered, but God gave the increase. So then neither he who plants is anything, nor he who waters, but God who gives the increase." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]

### Practical Application
This week, write down the names of three people you know who do not know Christ. Beside each name, write one thing you genuinely appreciate about them — their sense of humor, their kindness, their hard work. Pray for them by name each day, thanking God for that quality and asking Him to open a door for spiritual conversation. Before the week ends, initiate a conversation with at least one of them using one of the openers or transitions you have practiced in previous lessons. After the conversation, write down one thing you learned and one prayer for that person going forward.

### Summary
- Love for God and love for people is the only motivation strong enough to sustain evangelism over a lifetime.
- The love of Christ compels us to speak, while freeing us from the burden of results — we sow, God gives the growth.
- The cross is both the model and the message: Christ came to us while we were still enemies, and we go to others the same way.
