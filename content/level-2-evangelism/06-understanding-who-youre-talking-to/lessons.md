# Lessons — Understanding Who You're Talking To

---

## Lesson 1 — Why Understanding Your Audience Matters

### HOOK

Imagine walking into a room full of people and saying the exact same sentence to every single person, expecting each one to respond the same way. You would not do that with a friend, a child, a stranger, or someone who is angry with you. So why do so many believers share the gospel as though every person they meet has the same background, the same beliefs, and the same heart condition?

### CORE TEACHING

Every person you speak to about Christ arrives at that conversation with a lifetime of experiences, beliefs, assumptions, and wounds. Some grew up in church but have no personal faith. Others have been burned by religion and want nothing to do with God. Some are proud of their morality and see no need for a Saviour. Others are crushed by their sin and desperately looking for hope. The same gospel message does not change, but the way you help a person see their need for it must be tailored to where they actually are. This is what it means to understand who you are talking to. Jesus Himself demonstrated this perfectly. To the self-righteous Pharisee, He used the law to expose sin. To the woman at the well, He gently revealed that He knew her entire life story. To the rich young ruler, He put His finger on the one thing the man loved more than God. In every case, Jesus knew exactly what was in the person's heart before He spoke. You do not have that supernatural knowledge, but you can develop the discipline of discernment through listening, asking the right questions, and paying attention to how a person responds. The apostle Paul modeled this when he walked into Athens and observed their altar to the Unknown God before he ever opened his mouth. He started where they were, not where he wished they were. Understanding your audience is not manipulation. It is love. It is refusing to spray the gospel like a firehose at every face that passes by, and instead taking the time to know what is already going on inside a person so that the truth can reach the heart instead of bouncing off the walls of pride or confusion. This module will equip you with the tools to discern what kind of person you are talking to, so you can speak the truth in a way they can actually hear.

### KEY SCRIPTURES

- 1 Samuel 16:7 — "For the Lord does not see as man sees; for man looks at the outward appearance, but the Lord looks at the heart." (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Acts 17:22–23 — "Then Paul stood in the midst of the Areopagus and said, 'Men of Athens, I perceive that in all things you are very religious; for as I was passing through and considering the objects of your worship, I even found an altar with this inscription: TO THE UNKNOWN GOD. Therefore, the One whom you worship without knowing, Him I proclaim to you.'" (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Proverbs 18:13 — "He who answers a matter before he hears it, it is folly and shame to him." (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Colossians 4:5–6 — "Walk in wisdom toward those who are outside, redeeming the time. Let your speech always be with grace, seasoned with salt, that you may know how you ought to answer each one." (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]

### PRACTICAL APPLICATION

This week, before you share the gospel with anyone, spend at least ten minutes in conversation learning about their background. Ask them where they grew up, what they believe about God, and whether they have ever had a negative experience with religion. Do not try to correct them or preach yet — just listen and take notes mentally. Write down what you learn afterward.

### SUMMARY

- Every person has a unique background and heart condition that affects how they hear the gospel.
- Jesus and Paul both modelled the practice of understanding the listener before speaking.
- The goal of understanding your audience is love, not manipulation — it helps the truth reach the heart.

## Lesson 2 — Three Kinds of People You Will Meet

### HOOK

Think about the last three people you spoke with. One may have told you they were a Christian but never go to church. Another may have said they believe in God but think all religions lead to the same place. A third may have told you they do not believe any of it. You would not talk to these three people the same way — but do you know exactly how to tell which is which before you start?

### CORE TEACHING

Most people you share the gospel with will fall into one of three broad categories: nominal Christians, religious people, and sceptics. A nominal Christian is someone who identifies as Christian by culture, family tradition, or nationality but has never personally repented and trusted Christ. They may believe in God, know some Bible stories, and even feel defensive about Christianity, but they have no living relationship with Jesus. Their biggest danger is that they think they are already saved. Religious people include those who are devout in another faith — Muslims, Jehovah's Witnesses, Mormons, Hindus, or anyone who is sincere in a system of belief that denies the gospel of grace. They often know their religion well and can argue from it. They need to see that their good works cannot save them and that Jesus is the only way. Sceptics are those who do not believe — atheists, agnostics, or people who are angry at God or the church. They may be proud in their unbelief or genuinely searching but unable to get past intellectual or emotional barriers. The sceptic often needs the law to expose their sin and humble their pride before they can see their need for grace. How do you tell the difference? The proud person will usually dismiss the idea of an afterlife, insist they are a good person, and show no fear of death. The humble person will admit they are not perfect, express uncertainty about what happens after death, and demonstrate openness to listen. The nominal Christian will speak about God in familiar, casual terms without any evidence of personal conviction or transformation. Learning to identify these three types is the first skill of effective evangelism. It keeps you from wasting time arguing with a nominal Christian about things they think they already know, or offering grace to a proud sceptic who has not yet felt the weight of their sin.

### KEY SCRIPTURES

- 1 Corinthians 2:14 — "But the natural man does not receive the things of the Spirit of God, for they are foolishness to him; nor can he know them, because they are spiritually discerned." (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Jude 1:22–23 — "And on some have compassion, making a distinction; but others save with fear, pulling them out of the fire, hating even the garment defiled by the flesh." (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Acts 13:41 — "Behold, you despisers, marvel and perish! For I work a work in your days, a work which you will by no means believe, though one were to declare it to you." (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Matthew 7:15 — "Beware of false prophets, who come to you in sheep's clothing, but inwardly they are ravenous wolves." (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]

### PRACTICAL APPLICATION

This week, approach three people with the simple question: "Do you think there is an afterlife?" Listen to their answer and note whether they sound certain, uncertain, proud, humble, dismissive, or open. Based on their response, categorize them as nominal, religious, or sceptic. Do not try to share the full gospel yet — just practice identifying the type of person.

### SUMMARY

- Nominal Christians have cultural Christianity but no personal conversion; they think they are saved when they may not be.
- Religious people trust in a system of works or a different gospel; they need to see their righteousness is insufficient.
- Sceptics are proud or wounded in their unbelief; they often need the law to prepare their hearts for grace.

## Lesson 3 — True Conversion and False Conversion

### HOOK

A man prays a prayer at a crusade, raises his hand, and walks forward. He is glowing with emotion. Everyone around him celebrates. Three months later, he is living exactly as he did before, with no hunger for God, no love for Scripture, and no change in his life. Was he saved? The answer to that question determines everything about how you evaluate an evangelistic encounter.

### CORE TEACHING

Jesus gave us the Parable of the Sower in Mark chapter 4 to explain a painful reality: not everyone who hears the gospel is genuinely converted. The seed is the same. The sower is the same. But the soil determines the outcome. The stony ground hearer receives the word with joy immediately, but because there is no depth of root, when tribulation or persecution arises because of the word, they fall away. This person had an emotional response but no true repentance. The thorny ground hearer also receives the word, but the cares of this world, the deceitfulness of riches, and the desires for other things choke the word, and it becomes unfruitful. This person had an intellectual agreement but no ongoing fruit. Only the good soil hearer hears the word, accepts it, and bears fruit — some thirty, some sixty, some one hundredfold. A false convert may believe for a season. They may have joy, excitement, and even a genuine emotional experience. But they lack root — they have not been convicted of their sin by the law of God, so they have never truly repented. What exposes them? Three things: tribulation (trials and difficulties), temptation (opportunities to sin), and persecution (rejection for their faith). When the sun of these things beats down, the false convert withers while the true convert grows deeper roots. A genuine convert, on the other hand, brings forth fruit over time. Jesus said you will know them by their fruits. This fruit includes repentance — a real turning from sin; thankfulness — a grateful heart for what God has done; good works — a life that serves others; the fruit of the Spirit — love, joy, peace, patience, kindness, goodness; and righteousness — doing what is right according to God's standard, not man's. Understanding true versus false conversion changes how you evangelize. It makes you unwilling to manipulate someone into a quick decision, and it compels you to use the law of God to bring genuine conviction of sin so that when a person does come to Christ, their faith is built on the rock, not on sand.

### KEY SCRIPTURES

- Mark 4:3–8, 16–20 — "Listen! Behold, a sower went out to sow... These likewise are the ones sown on stony ground who, when they hear the word, immediately receive it with gladness; and they have no root in themselves, and so endure only for a time. Afterward, when tribulation or persecution arises for the word's sake, immediately they stumble... Now these are the ones sown on good ground, those who hear the word, accept it, and bear fruit: some thirtyfold, some sixty, and some a hundred." (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Matthew 7:16–20 — "You will know them by their fruits. Do men gather grapes from thornbushes or figs from thistles? Even so, every good tree bears good fruit, but a bad tree bears bad fruit." (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Romans 3:20 — "Therefore by the deeds of the law no flesh will be justified in His sight, for by the law is the knowledge of sin." (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Galatians 3:24 — "Therefore the law was our tutor to bring us to Christ, that we might be justified by faith." (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]

### PRACTICAL APPLICATION

This week, take fifteen minutes to read Matthew 7:15–23 slowly and ask yourself: Based on the fruit in your own life — not your words or your past decisions — is there evidence of genuine conversion? Write down three specific fruits you have seen God produce in your life since you believed. If you struggle to identify any, do not assume — ask God to search your heart.

### SUMMARY

- The Parable of the Sower reveals that not everyone who responds to the gospel is genuinely converted.
- False converts may show initial joy and belief but fall away under tribulation, temptation, or persecution.
- True converts bear lasting fruit: repentance, thankfulness, good works, the fruit of the Spirit, and righteousness.

## Lesson 4 — Asking Diagnostic Questions

### HOOK

A doctor would never prescribe medicine before diagnosing the illness. A mechanic would never fix a car without first finding out what is wrong. Yet many believers jump straight into presenting the gospel without first understanding what is actually going on in the person's heart and mind. A few simple questions can change everything.

### CORE TEACHING

Diagnostic questions are strategic questions that help you discern a person's spiritual condition, their level of pride or humility, and what they actually believe before you begin sharing the gospel. They are not designed to trap or embarrass anyone. They are designed to open a window into the soul so you know how to proceed. Three of the most effective diagnostic questions are these: First, "Do you think there is an afterlife?" This question reveals what a person believes about eternity. The proud sceptic will often dismiss the question or say nobody can know. The humble seeker will express uncertainty and openness. The nominal Christian may say yes but show no urgency about it. Second, "Do you consider yourself a good person?" This is the most revealing question of all. If a person says yes without hesitation, they are likely self-righteous and need to be confronted with the law. If they hesitate or admit they have done wrong, they may already be open to grace. Third, "Are you afraid of dying?" Fear of death is a God-given alarm system. A person who is not afraid may be either genuinely secure in Christ or dangerously numb to eternity. A person who is afraid has a conscience that is still working. These questions do more than gather information — they also prepare the heart. When a person who thinks they are good is shown the Ten Commandments and realizes they are a liar, a thief, a blasphemer, and an adulterer at heart, their self-righteousness crumbles. That is when the gospel becomes good news instead of an optional upgrade. Jesus used this approach with the rich young ruler, and Paul implied it when he said that by the law is the knowledge of sin. The law is not the gospel, but it is the tool God uses to drive a sinner to Christ. Asking diagnostic questions is simply the way you apply that tool with wisdom and love. It is the difference between throwing a life preserver to someone who does not know they are drowning and helping them realize they are in the water before they will ever reach out for help.

### KEY SCRIPTURES

- Romans 3:19–20 — "Now we know that whatever the law says, it says to those who are under the law, that every mouth may be stopped, and all the world may become guilty before God. Therefore by the deeds of the law no flesh will be justified in His sight, for by the law is the knowledge of sin." (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Proverbs 20:5 — "Counsel in the heart of man is like deep water, but a man of understanding will draw it out." (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- 1 Corinthians 1:27 — "But God has chosen the foolish things of the world to put to shame the wise, and God has chosen the weak things of the world to put to shame the things which are mighty." (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- James 4:6 — "But He gives more grace. Therefore He says: 'God resists the proud, But gives grace to the humble.'" (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]

### PRACTICAL APPLICATION

This week, memorize the three diagnostic questions: "Do you think there is an afterlife?", "Do you consider yourself a good person?", and "Are you afraid of dying?" Practice asking them in a normal conversation with at least one person. After they answer, do not jump into the gospel yet. Simply listen and note what their answers reveal about their heart condition.

### SUMMARY

- Diagnostic questions reveal a person's worldview, level of pride or humility, and readiness for the gospel.
- The most revealing question is "Do you consider yourself a good person?" — it exposes self-righteousness or openness.
- These questions also prepare the heart by awakening the conscience through the law of God.

## Lesson 5 — Tailoring Your Approach

### HOOK

Imagine two people standing in front of you. One has just been diagnosed with a terminal illness and knows they are going to die. The other has just won the lottery and thinks life could not get any better. You have the cure for both of them, but telling them the same way will not work. One needs urgent warning; the other needs to see why they are actually dying.

### CORE TEACHING

The apostle Paul said he became all things to all people that by all possible means he might save some. He did not mean he changed the gospel — he meant he adapted his method, his tone, and his entry point based on who he was talking to. To the Jews, he started with the Scriptures they already trusted. To the pagans in Athens, he started with their own poets and their altar to an unknown God. He did not preach the same sermon to every audience. He tailored his approach. This is the skill of cutting your cloth to fit the person. When you are dealing with a proud sceptic, the best approach is to use the law of God to bring the knowledge of sin. Ask them the diagnostic questions, walk them through the Ten Commandments, and let their own conscience convict them. The law exposes their self-righteousness and shows them they are not as good as they think. Only then does the gospel become relevant. When you are dealing with a nominal Christian, the approach is different. They already believe in God and think they are saved. You need to gently help them examine whether their faith is real. Ask them what the gospel is, what repentance means to them, and whether their life has changed since they "became a Christian." When you are dealing with a religious person from another faith, you need to understand enough of their beliefs to show them where their trust is misplaced. A Jehovah's Witness trusts the Watchtower; a Muslim trusts his good deeds and Allah's mercy. In both cases, you must lovingly show that only Christ's finished work on the cross can save. With the humble and broken, you do not need the law — they already know they are sinners. Move quickly to grace. Tell them about the cross, the resurrection, and the free gift of salvation. The key principle is this: God resists the proud but gives grace to the humble. So should you. The proud receive a warning; the humble receive mercy. Tailoring your approach is not about being tricky. It is about being wise. It is about applying the right tool for the right situation so that the power of the gospel can do its work.

### KEY SCRIPTURES

- 1 Corinthians 9:19–22 — "For though I am free from all men, I have made myself a servant to all, that I might win the more; and to the Jews I became as a Jew, that I might win Jews... to the weak I became as weak, that I might win the weak. I have become all things to all men, that I might by all means save some." (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- James 4:6 — "But He gives more grace. Therefore He says: 'God resists the proud, But gives grace to the humble.'" (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Matthew 10:16 — "Behold, I send you out as sheep in the midst of wolves. Therefore be wise as serpents and harmless as doves." (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- 2 Timothy 2:24–25 — "And a servant of the Lord must not quarrel but be gentle to all, able to teach, patient, in humility correcting those who are in opposition, if God perhaps will grant them repentance, so that they may know the truth." (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]

### PRACTICAL APPLICATION

This week, identify one person you already know who is either proud about their goodness or broken about their sin. Based on what they have told you, decide which approach you will use: the law to expose pride, or grace to comfort the broken. Have a conversation with them using that approach and see how they respond.

### SUMMARY

- Paul modelled adapting the method while keeping the message unchanged, becoming all things to all people.
- The proud need the law to expose their self-righteousness; the humble and broken need grace and the cross.
- The principle of James 4:6 — resist the proud, give grace to the humble — should guide every approach.

## Lesson 6 — Building Rapport and Earning Trust

### HOOK

Nobody opens their heart to a stranger who walks up and immediately starts telling them they are wrong. Before anyone will listen to what you have to say about their eternal soul, they need to know that you actually see them, hear them, and care about them as a person, not just as a project.

### CORE TEACHING

Rapport is the bridge that allows the truth to travel from your mouth to someone else's heart. Without it, even the most accurate gospel presentation will sound like noise. Building rapport does not mean compromising the message or avoiding hard truths. It means creating a context in which those hard truths can be received. The first step is genuine listening. Most people are so used to being talked at that when someone actually listens to them, it disarms their defences. Ask about their life, their family, their struggles, and their beliefs without interrupting or correcting. Let them finish. Let them feel heard. The second step is respect. You do not have to agree with someone to respect them as a person made in the image of God. You can disagree firmly and still speak with kindness. Jesus was full of both grace and truth. He never softened the truth, but He also never treated people as objects. The woman at the well was a Samaritan and an adulteress — two categories that most Jews would have used to dismiss her. But Jesus spoke to her with dignity, and she was transformed. The third step is honesty. Do not promise that following Jesus will make life easier. Do not tell someone that all their problems will disappear if they become a Christian. The apostle Paul promised the opposite — that through many tribulations we must enter the kingdom of God. When you are honest about the cost of discipleship, you build credibility. The person knows you are not selling them something. The fourth step is patience. Genuine conversion is the work of the Holy Spirit, not the result of your persuasive skills. You may plant a seed, another may water it, and God gives the increase. Do not pressure someone into a decision they do not understand. Give them room to think, to ask questions, and to count the cost. Building rapport also means maintaining the relationship even after the conversation ends. A Jehovah's Witness who has been approached with kindness and truth may eventually leave the Watchtower, but they need to know there is a Christian who will walk with them through that difficult transition. Your love after the conversation may be as powerful as your words during it.

### KEY SCRIPTURES

- John 4:7–10 — "A woman of Samaria came to draw water. Jesus said to her, 'Give Me a drink.' For His disciples had gone away into the city to buy food. Then the woman of Samaria said to Him, 'How is it that You, being a Jew, ask a drink from me, a Samaritan woman?' For Jews have no dealings with Samaritans." (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Colossians 4:6 — "Let your speech always be with grace, seasoned with salt, that you may know how you ought to answer each one." (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- 1 Thessalonians 5:21 — "Test all things; hold fast what is good." (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Proverbs 27:17 — "As iron sharpens iron, so a man sharpens the countenance of his friend." (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]

### PRACTICAL APPLICATION

This week, identify one person you know who does not share your faith. Spend at least twenty minutes with them in a conversation where your only goal is to listen and understand — no preaching, no correcting, no gospel presentation. Ask about their life, their struggles, and what they believe. At the end, thank them for talking to you.

### SUMMARY

- Rapport is built through genuine listening, respect, honesty about the cost, and patience for the Spirit's timing.
- Jesus modelled treating people with dignity regardless of their background or sin.
- Maintaining relationship after the conversation may be as important as the conversation itself.

## Lesson 7 — Reading Your Audience in Real Time

### HOOK

You have prepared. You have asked the right questions. You have identified the type of person you are talking to. But then, in the middle of the conversation, something changes — the person gets defensive, or they tear up, or they change the subject, or they ask a question you did not expect. What do you do now? The ability to read your audience in real time separates a rigid presenter from a Spirit-led witness.

### CORE TEACHING

No matter how well you prepare, every gospel conversation is a living interaction between two souls, and the Holy Spirit is at work in ways you cannot predict. Reading your audience in real time means paying attention to the subtle and not-so-subtle signals a person gives you during the conversation and adjusting accordingly. The first signal is defensiveness. If a person crosses their arms, looks away, or starts arguing, you may be dealing with pride or a wounded conscience. Do not push harder — that will only make them dig in. Instead, back up and ask another diagnostic question. "Does what I am saying bother you?" or "Have you had a bad experience with Christians before?" This can reveal the root of the resistance. The second signal is openness. If a person leans in, asks questions, becomes quiet and thoughtful, or starts admitting fault, the soil is being prepared. This is the moment to move toward the cross. Do not get distracted by secondary issues. Their conscience is awake; now is the time to point them to the Saviour. The third signal is distraction. If a person keeps checking their phone, looking around, or changing the subject to trivial things, you may be dealing with thorny ground hearers whose hearts are choked by the cares of the world. Do not force the conversation. Leave them with something to think about and pray that the Spirit will continue the work. The fourth signal is emotional response. Tears do not always mean repentance, and laughter does not always mean rejection. Some people cry because they are convicted; others cry because they are uncomfortable. Some people joke because they are nervous; others joke because they are mocking. Learn to distinguish between the two by the context of the rest of the conversation. The ultimate goal is not to deliver a perfect presentation but to be sensitive to what the Holy Spirit is doing. The Spirit convicts, and the Spirit draws. Your job is to cooperate with what He is already doing, not to manufacture a response through pressure or clever words. Trust that God is at work, keep your eyes open, and be ready to pivot at any moment.

### KEY SCRIPTURES

- Hebrews 4:12 — "For the word of God is living and powerful, and sharper than any two-edged sword, piercing even to the division of soul and spirit, and of joints and marrow, and is a discerner of the thoughts and intents of the heart." (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- John 3:19–21 — "And this is the condemnation, that the light has come into the world, and men loved darkness rather than light, because their deeds were evil. For everyone practicing evil hates the light and does not come to the light, lest his deeds should be exposed." (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- 1 Corinthians 3:6–7 — "I planted, Apollos watered, but God gave the increase. So then neither he who plants is anything, nor he who waters, but God who gives the increase." (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Matthew 13:23 — "But he who received seed on the good ground is he who hears the word and understands it, who indeed bears fruit and produces: some a hundredfold, some sixty, some thirty." (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]

### PRACTICAL APPLICATION

This week, during any spiritual conversation, challenge yourself to identify at least one real-time signal from the person you are speaking with — defensiveness, openness, distraction, or emotion. Based on that signal, adjust what you say next. If they are defensive, back up. If they are open, move toward the cross. Write down what happened.

### SUMMARY

- Reading your audience in real time means watching for defensiveness, openness, distraction, and emotional response.
- Defensive people need space; open people need the cross; distracted people need a seed planted for later.
- The Holy Spirit is the one who convicts and draws — your job is to cooperate with His work, not manufacture results.
