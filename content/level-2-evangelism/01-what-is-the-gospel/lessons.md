# Lessons: What Is the Gospel?

---

## Lesson 1: The Gospel Begins with God

### Hook
A judge who lets a guilty criminal go free to avoid personal discomfort is called corrupt. On the other hand, a judge who condemns a guilty criminal but then steps down from the bench and pays the fine himself is doing something remarkable. That picture begins to show us what the gospel is — but to understand it, we must first understand the God who is at the center of it. The gospel does not begin with man, his needs, or his felt wants. It begins with God.

### Core Teaching
The gospel begins with God because God is the central figure of the entire biblical narrative. Before we can understand our sin, we must first understand the holiness of God. Holy means separate — God is separate from sin, perfect in purity, and utterly unique in His being. The seraphim in Isaiah's vision cried out, "Holy, holy, holy is the Lord of hosts," and the earth shook at His presence. This is not merely one attribute among many; it is the atmosphere in which all of God's other attributes exist. His love is holy love, His mercy is holy mercy, His wrath is holy wrath. God is also just. Because He is holy, He cannot overlook sin or pretend it does not matter. His justice demands that sin be punished. Every wrong must be made right, every violation of His law must be addressed. This is not cruelty — it is moral perfection. A god who did not punish evil would not be good. At the same time, God is love. But His love is not a sentimental feeling that overrides His justice. It is a love that acts to save sinners in a way that fully satisfies the demands of His justice. The cross is where God's love and God's justice meet. Finally, God is the Creator. He made us, and He owns us. We did not create ourselves and we do not belong to ourselves. Every human being exists for God's glory and is accountable to Him. The gospel begins here — not with what we want but with who God is. A gospel that starts with man's felt needs rather than God's glory is a different gospel altogether. The true gospel calls us to see God for who He really is and to respond accordingly.

### Key Scriptures
- Isaiah 6:1-5 — "Holy, holy, holy is the LORD of hosts; the whole earth is full of His glory!" [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Psalm 99:9 — "Exalt the LORD our God, and worship at His holy hill; for the LORD our God is holy." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Deuteronomy 32:4 — "For all His ways are justice, a God of truth and without injustice; righteous and upright is He." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- 1 John 4:8 — "God is love." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]

### Practical Application
This week, set aside fifteen minutes each day to read one psalm (start with Psalms 33, 99, 103, and 145) and write down one attribute of God that stands out from that day's reading. After seven days, review what you have written and ask: "Does the way I speak about God to others reflect this God, or a smaller version I have created?" Then identify one conversation this coming week where you will intentionally mention God's holiness or justice rather than focusing only on His love.

### Summary
The gospel begins with God, not with man. God is holy, just, and loving — and these attributes are not in tension but perfectly integrated in His character. We cannot understand the good news of what Christ has done until we first understand the God before whom we stand.

## Lesson 2: The Bad News — Man's Condition

### Hook
Imagine a person floating peacefully on their back in a lake, staring at the clouds, completely relaxed. What they do not know is that they are floating in acid that is slowly dissolving their skin. They feel no pain because the acid has already destroyed their nerve endings. Their calm is not peace — it is death. According to Scripture, this is the spiritual condition of every human being apart from grace.

### Core Teaching
The gospel must include bad news because the good news only makes sense against the backdrop of our true condition. Scripture teaches that all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God. To sin is not merely to make mistakes or to fail to live up to our potential — it is to rebel against the living God, to transgress His holy law, and to choose our own will over His. The heart of sin is not immoral behavior but self-worship: we have exchanged the glory of God for images of our own making. This sin is not a surface problem. The Bible describes humanity as dead in trespasses and sins. Dead people cannot respond to anything. A corpse cannot hear a command, feel an appeal, or decide to start breathing. Spiritual death means we are not sick — we are dead. We do not need resuscitation; we need resurrection. Furthermore, by nature, all are children of wrath. This is not because of what we do but because of what we are. We sin because we are sinners by nature. The heart is deceitful above all things and desperately wicked, and no one naturally seeks after God. The law was given not to save us but to show us our sin, to silence every excuse, and to hold the whole world guilty before God. Like a mirror, the law reveals the dirt — it does not clean it. When a person truly sees themselves in light of God's holy law, they stop comparing themselves to others and begin crying out for mercy. Until a person understands the bad news, the good news will never seem good to them. A gospel preached without the bad news produces superficial conversions — people who think they are fine and that Jesus came to make their lives a little better. But the gospel is not an improvement program; it is a rescue mission for the dead.

### Key Scriptures
- Romans 3:10-12 — "There is none righteous, no, not one; there is none who understands; there is none who seeks after God." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Ephesians 2:1-3 — "And you He made alive, who were dead in trespasses and sins... and were by nature children of wrath." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Jeremiah 17:9 — "The heart is deceitful above all things, and desperately wicked; who can know it?" [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Romans 3:19-20 — "that every mouth may be stopped, and all the world may become guilty before God." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]

### Practical Application
This week, read Romans 1:18 through Romans 3:20 in a single sitting. Then take a sheet of paper and write down every phrase Paul uses to describe humanity's condition. Do not rush to the good news — stay with the diagnosis until you feel its weight. Then ask one trusted believer: "Where do I still minimize or excuse my own sin?" Commit to bringing that specific area before God in confession each day this week, naming it explicitly rather than using general language.

### Summary
Humanity is not sick but dead, not lost but guilty, not struggling but rebellious. The bad news must be understood and felt before the good news can be truly received.

### Next Lesson Preview
If our condition is as desperate as this, then what hope is there? God did not give His law merely to condemn us — He gave it to drive us to Christ. Next: The Law and the Gospel.

## Lesson 3: The Law and the Gospel

### Hook
A parachute is useless to someone who does not believe they are falling. As long as a person is convinced their feet are firmly on the ground, they will refuse to put on the harness. The same is true of the gospel — those who do not know they are under judgment see no need for a Savior. This is why God gave the law: not to save, but to show us that we are falling.

### Core Teaching
The law of God serves a critical purpose in the gospel, yet it is widely misunderstood. The law was never given as a means of salvation. If salvation could come through the law, then Christ died for nothing. The law was given for several purposes that work together to drive us to the gospel. First, the law shows us God's standard. The commands of God are not suggestions — they reveal the holiness of God and the righteousness He requires. The law demands perfect obedience in thought, word, and deed, and it does not lower its standards for anyone. Second, the law stops every mouth. When a person begins to compare themselves to God's law, all self-justification ends. The law does not allow for excuses or comparisons with other people. It holds up the perfect standard of God and shows every person exactly where they stand — guilty before a holy God. Third, the law serves as a tutor to lead us to Christ. Once we see our guilt and helplessness under the law, we are driven to look outside ourselves for a Savior. The law shows us the disease; the gospel provides the cure. The law diagnoses; the gospel heals. The law condemns; the gospel pardons. But neither can do the work of the other. A common error is to mix the law and the gospel — to say that we are saved by grace but kept by works, or that Christ paid most of the penalty but we must pay the rest through our efforts. The gospel is that Christ fully satisfied the demands of the law on behalf of sinners. He lived the perfect life the law requires, and He died the death the law demands. Those who trust in Him are not under the law as a means of salvation but are united to Christ, in whom the law's demands have been fully met. The law still serves as a guide for the believer's life, revealing what pleases God, but it no longer stands over us as a condemning judge.

### Key Scriptures
- Galatians 3:24-25 — "Therefore the law was our tutor to bring us to Christ, that we might be justified by faith." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Romans 3:19-20 — "by the deeds of the law no flesh will be justified in His sight, for by the law is the knowledge of sin." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Romans 7:7 — "I would not have known sin except through the law." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Galatians 2:16 — "knowing that a man is not justified by the works of the law but by faith in Jesus Christ." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]

### Practical Application
This week, take the Ten Commandments from Exodus 20 and write each one in your own words. Beneath each command, write one specific way you have broken it — not comparing yourself to others but honestly before God. After you finish, read Romans 8:1 aloud: "There is therefore now no condemnation to those who are in Christ Jesus." Then destroy the list as a reminder that Christ has fully paid for every charge the law could bring against you. Repeat this exercise with one other person this month to help them see the purpose of the law.

### Summary
The law was never meant to save — it was meant to show us our need for a Savior. It diagnoses the disease but cannot cure it. The gospel announces that Christ has fully satisfied the law on behalf of all who trust in Him.

## Lesson 4: The Heart of the Gospel — Christ's Substitutionary Work

### Hook
If a man owes a debt he cannot pay and someone else pays it for him, the debt is cancelled. But if a man is guilty of a crime and someone else takes his place in prison, justice is still served because the punishment was carried out. What Christ did on the cross is greater than both of these pictures combined: He paid a debt we owed and took a punishment we deserved, and He did it as our substitute.

### Core Teaching
At the center of the gospel stands the cross of Jesus Christ. Not a cross as a piece of jewelry or a religious symbol, but the actual historical event in which the Son of God died as a substitute for sinners. This is not a side doctrine or an optional emphasis — it is the heart of the Christian faith. Jesus Christ, the eternal Son of God, took on human flesh. He was fully God and fully man. He lived a life of perfect obedience to the law of God, doing what no other human being has ever done. Then, in the climax of His earthly ministry, He was crucified under Pontius Pilate. But His death was not merely the martyrdom of a good teacher — it was a substitutionary sacrifice. The Bible says that God made Him who knew no sin to be sin for us. This means that on the cross, Christ was treated by God as if He had committed every sin of every person who would ever believe in Him. The full weight of divine wrath that should have fallen on sinners was poured out on Him. He became a curse for us. He was pierced for our transgressions and crushed for our iniquities. This is what theologians call propitiation — the turning away of God's wrath through the sacrifice of Christ. God did not simply overlook sin; He punished it fully in the person of His Son. Justice was not set aside — it was satisfied. Three days later, Christ rose from the dead. The resurrection is God's declaration that the sacrifice was accepted, that sin has been atoned for, and that death has been defeated. If Christ had only died, we would have no assurance that His work was sufficient. But because He rose, we know that the Father accepted the Son's sacrifice and that all who are in Christ are fully forgiven. The cross and resurrection together are the non-negotiable foundation of the gospel. Remove either one and there is no good news.

### Key Scriptures
- 2 Corinthians 5:21 — "For He made Him who knew no sin to be sin for us, that we might become the righteousness of God in Him." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Isaiah 53:5-6 — "He was wounded for our transgressions, He was bruised for our iniquities; the chastisement for our peace was upon Him, and by His stripes we are healed." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- 1 Peter 3:18 — "For Christ also suffered once for sins, the just for the unjust, that He might bring us to God." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- 1 Corinthians 15:3-4 — "Christ died for our sins according to the Scriptures, and that He was buried, and that He rose again the third day according to the Scriptures." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Romans 3:25-26 — "whom God set forth as a propitiation by His blood, through faith, to demonstrate His righteousness." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]

### Practical Application
This week, memorize 2 Corinthians 5:21. Write it on a card and carry it with you. Each day, read it slowly three times, substituting "me" and "my" for the plural pronouns: "He made Him who knew no sin to be sin for me, that I might become the righteousness of God in Him." Before you go to sleep each night, thank God specifically that Christ bore the specific sins you committed that day. Schedule a thirty-minute window this weekend to sit alone and slowly read the account of the crucifixion from one of the Gospels, pausing after each verse to ask: "He did this for me."

### Summary
The heart of the gospel is Jesus Christ's substitutionary death and victorious resurrection. On the cross, God's justice and mercy meet — sin is fully punished and sinners are fully pardoned in the person of the Son.

## Lesson 5: The Gift of Repentance

### Hook
A doctor can offer a cure, but the patient must stop drinking the poison. If a man is told that his disease is caused by what he is consuming and he refuses to stop consuming it, the cure will do him no good. In the same way, the gospel calls us not only to receive Christ but also to turn from sin. This turning is called repentance, and it is not optional.

### Core Teaching
Repentance is one of the two necessary responses to the gospel, and it is a gift from God. Scripture says that God commands all people everywhere to repent, and that He grants repentance to those who believe. Repentance is not merely feeling sorry for sin — many people feel sorry when they get caught. Repentance is a change of mind that leads to a change of direction. It is turning from sin and self-reliance and turning toward God. True repentance involves three elements. First, there is an intellectual element — a recognition that sin is what God says it is: rebellion against Him. This is not merely admitting that we have made mistakes but agreeing with God's assessment that our sin is evil, offensive, and deserving of judgment. Second, there is an emotional element — a genuine sorrow over sin, not because of its consequences but because of what it is: an offense against a holy and loving God. This is not the sorrow of the world that leads to despair but a sorrow that leads to life. Third, there is a volitional element — a deliberate turning from sin to God. This is not perfection but direction. A truly repentant person does not continue living in sin without conflict. They may still stumble, but they no longer make peace with their sin. They fight against it. Repentance is not a one-time event at the beginning of the Christian life — it is a posture that continues throughout life. Every day, the believer turns from sin and turns to God. Martin Luther famously said that the entire Christian life is repentance. It is important to understand that repentance is not a human work that earns salvation. It is a gift that God gives, a grace that enables us to turn. But it is also a command that we must obey. We cannot repent apart from God's grace, but we are still responsible to repent. The two truths stand together: repent, for the kingdom of heaven is at hand.

### Key Scriptures
- Acts 17:30 — "Truly, these times of ignorance God overlooked, but now commands all men everywhere to repent." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Acts 11:18 — "God has also granted to the Gentiles repentance to life." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- 2 Corinthians 7:10 — "For godly sorrow produces repentance leading to salvation, not to be regretted." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Acts 3:19 — "Repent therefore and be converted, that your sins may be blotted out." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Ezekiel 36:31 — "Then you will remember your evil ways and your deeds that were not good; and you will loathe yourselves in your own sight for your iniquities and your abominations." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]

### Practical Application
This week, sit down with a piece of paper and draw a line down the middle. On the left side, list every sin you are currently making peace with — not fighting against, not confessing, but tolerating in your life. On the right side, write a specific step you will take this week to turn from each one. If you cannot think of a step for a particular sin, ask a trusted believer for help. Choose one item from the right side and take that step today, not tomorrow. At the end of the week, repeat the exercise and see whether any items from the left have been removed or added.

### Summary
Repentance is a gift from God and a command from God. It is a turning from sin to God that involves the mind, the emotions, and the will. It is not a single event but a lifelong posture.

## Lesson 6: The Gift of Faith

### Hook
Imagine a drowning man who is thrown a rope. He cannot save himself — the current is too strong, and he has no strength left. But the rope has been thrown, and the one holding it is strong enough to pull him in. What must the drowning man do? He must let go of everything else and take hold of the rope. He must stop thrashing and grasping at debris. He must trust the rope and the one who holds it. This is faith — not doing something to earn rescue but receiving rescue by taking hold of the Rescuer.

### Core Teaching
Faith is the second essential response to the gospel, and like repentance, it is both a command and a gift. The gospel announces what Christ has done, and faith is the hand that receives it. Faith is not a vague optimism or a general belief that God exists — even the demons believe that, and they tremble. Saving faith has three distinct elements. First, there is knowledge — the content of the gospel must be understood. A person cannot believe what they do not know. This is why the gospel must be preached: faith comes by hearing, and hearing by the word of God. Second, there is assent — the mind must agree that the gospel is true. It is not enough to know the facts; a person must be convinced that Jesus Christ is the Son of God, that He died for sinners, and that He rose from the dead. But even this, by itself, is not saving faith. Third, there is trust — the will must act upon that knowledge and assent by resting upon Christ alone for salvation. This is the element that makes faith saving. It is not merely believing that Jesus is the Son of God but believing in Him; not merely believing that He died for sinners but trusting that His death is sufficient for my sins. Faith is the opposite of works. It says, "I cannot save myself; I am not good enough, and I never will be. But Christ is good enough, and His work is sufficient for me." Faith looks away from self and looks to Christ. It is not the strength of faith that saves but the object of faith. Even weak faith in a strong Savior is enough. The smallest hand can receive the greatest gift. It is also important to understand that faith is not a human achievement. It is a gift of God — not of works, lest anyone should boast. Yet it is also a genuine act of the human will. God does not believe for us, but He gives us the ability to believe. Saving faith always produces fruit. True faith is not alone — it is accompanied by repentance and results in a changed life. Faith that is alone is not faith at all.

### Key Scriptures
- Ephesians 2:8-9 — "For by grace you have been saved through faith, and that not of yourselves; it is the gift of God, not of works, lest anyone should boast." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Romans 10:9-10 — "if you confess with your mouth the Lord Jesus and believe in your heart that God has raised Him from the dead, you will be saved." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Romans 10:17 — "So then faith comes by hearing, and hearing by the word of God." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- John 3:16 — "For God so loved the world that He gave His only begotten Son, that whoever believes in Him should not perish but have everlasting life." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Acts 16:31 — "Believe on the Lord Jesus Christ, and you will be saved." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]

### Practical Application
This week, write out a one-paragraph personal testimony of your faith that includes: (1) what you once trusted in for your standing before God, (2) when you first understood that you could not save yourself, and (3) what you now trust in — specifically and exclusively — for your salvation. Read it aloud to yourself each morning. Then share it with one person this week who has never heard it. If you realize you cannot confidently say what you trust in, set aside two hours this weekend to read through the gospel of John and ask God to give you saving faith.

### Summary
Faith is receiving Christ and resting in Him alone for salvation. It involves knowledge, assent, and personal trust. It is not a work but a gift — the empty hand that takes what Christ offers.

## Lesson 7: The Evidence of New Birth

### Hook
A man can hold a forged banknote in his hand for years, believing it is genuine. His belief does not make it real. But if he holds a genuine banknote, its authenticity is proven not by how tightly he grips it but by the markings that show it came from the proper authority. In the same way, a person can believe they are saved and be sincerely wrong. The question is not whether we believe we are saved but whether the marks of genuine salvation are present in our lives.

### Core Teaching
The new birth — regeneration — is the supernatural work of God by which He imparts new spiritual life to a dead soul. It is not something a person achieves but something God does. Jesus told Nicodemus that unless a person is born again, they cannot even see the kingdom of God, let alone enter it. Regeneration is compared to the wind: we cannot control it or fully understand it, but we can see its effects. What are the effects of regeneration? The new birth produces evidence that, while not perfect, is real and observable. First, there is a changed relationship to sin. A person who has been born again cannot continue living in sin as a settled pattern of life. They still sin, but they no longer make peace with it. Sin becomes their enemy rather than their friend. They confess it, fight it, and grieve over it. Second, there is a changed relationship to God. The regenerated person no longer sees God as a distant judge to be feared but as a loving Father to be cherished. They cry out to Him, "Abba, Father." They desire to know Him, to please Him, and to be with Him. Third, there is a changed relationship to other believers. The new birth produces love for the people of God. Those who have been born again are drawn to other believers, not because they are perfect but because they share the same life. Fourth, there is perseverance. Those who are truly born again will not ultimately fall away. They may stumble, they may wander, but God will bring them back. They are kept by the power of God through faith. This does not mean that every professing believer will persevere. Many who start well fall away, and their falling away demonstrates that they were never truly born again. They went out from us because they were not of us. Fifth, there is a love for the truth. The regenerated person loves the gospel. They do not grow tired of hearing about Christ. The same message that saved them continues to sustain them. Jesus spoke of a narrow gate and a narrow way that leads to life, and few find it. This is a sobering truth. Not everyone who says "Lord, Lord" will enter the kingdom. The call to examine ourselves is not optional — it is a command. But examination is not meant to produce doubt in the genuinely saved; it is meant to expose the falsely confident. Those who are truly in Christ can have assurance, not because they are perfect but because the Holy Spirit bears witness with their spirit that they are children of God.

### Key Scriptures
- John 3:3-8 — "Most assuredly, I say to you, unless one is born again, he cannot see the kingdom of God." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Ezekiel 36:26-27 — "I will give you a new heart and put a new spirit within you; I will take the heart of stone out of your flesh and give you a heart of flesh." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- 1 John 3:9 — "Whoever has been born of God does not sin, for His seed remains in him; and he cannot sin, because he has been born of God." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- 1 John 2:19 — "They went out from us, but they were not of us; for if they had been of us, they would have continued with us." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Romans 8:16 — "The Spirit Himself bears witness with our spirit that we are children of God." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Matthew 7:13-14 — "Enter by the narrow gate; for wide is the gate and broad is the way that leads to destruction." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- 2 Corinthians 13:5 — "Examine yourselves as to whether you are in the faith." [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]

### Practical Application
This week, work through the "tests of life" found in 1 John. Read one chapter each day for five days. After each chapter, write down one question the chapter asks of your life. For example: "Do I walk in the light or claim to have fellowship while walking in darkness?" "Do I love my brothers and sisters in practical ways?" "Do I confess the Son?" At the end of the week, do not ask "Am I perfect?" but "Is there evidence of new life?" If you find no evidence, do not manufacture assurance — go to a mature believer or an elder and share honestly what you have found. Schedule a specific time this month to have that conversation.

### Summary
The new birth is God's work of making dead sinners alive in Christ. It produces real, observable evidence: a changed relationship to sin, to God, to believers, and to truth. The narrow way is real, but so is the assurance that Christ gives to His people.
