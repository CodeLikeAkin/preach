## Lesson 1 — Why the Bible Can Be Trusted

### HOOK
You are holding a book that was written over the span of 1,500 years by more than 40 authors from vastly different backgrounds — kings, fishermen, a doctor, a tax collector, a tentmaker — most of whom never met one another. Yet they tell one seamless story. That alone makes the Bible unlike any other book in human history. But is it enough to trust it with your life?

### CORE TEACHING
Most Christians, when asked why they believe the Bible, give one of two answers: "I was raised that way" or "I tried it and it worked for me." Both answers are honest, but neither is sufficient. People are raised in many religions, and people try many things that change their lives — including false teachings. The question deserves a better answer.

The Bible itself gives us a better foundation. In 2 Peter chapter 1, an eyewitness to Jesus explains why he trusts what he wrote. He did not follow cleverly invented stories. He saw the majesty of Christ with his own eyes. He heard the voice of God with his own ears. And he points to the prophetic word as even more sure — Scripture that was written beforehand and later fulfilled in precise detail.

Think about how you trust anything you have not seen with your own eyes. You trust that a historical event happened because multiple eyewitnesses recorded it. You trust that a medicine works because it has been tested and documented. You trust that a map is accurate because others have followed it and arrived where it promised. The Bible invites this same kind of trust — not blind faith, but faith based on evidence.

The evidence for the Bible's reliability is staggering. Thousands of ancient manuscripts confirm its accuracy. Over 25,000 archaeological discoveries have corroborated its accounts. Hundreds of specific prophecies were fulfilled centuries after they were written. These facts do not save anyone — salvation is by grace through faith in Christ alone. But they answer the honest question: why this book and not another?

The purpose of this module is not just to give you arguments. It is to build a foundation underneath your faith so that when someone asks you why you believe, you have an answer — not a defensive one, but a confident one. The Bible is not a book you have to protect. It is a lion that can defend itself.

### KEY SCRIPTURES
- 2 Peter 1:16 — "For we did not follow cunningly devised fables when we made known to you the power and coming of our Lord Jesus Christ, but were eyewitnesses of His majesty." (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Luke 1:1-4 — Luke's prologue explains that he carefully investigated everything from the beginning and wrote an orderly account so that readers "may know the certainty of those things in which you were instructed." (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- 2 Timothy 3:16-17 — "All Scripture is given by inspiration of God, and is profitable for doctrine, for reproof, for correction, for instruction in righteousness, that the man of God may be complete, thoroughly equipped for every good work." (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]

### PRACTICAL APPLICATION
This week, take a piece of paper and write down the single biggest question you have ever had about whether the Bible can be trusted. Do not filter it. Write the real question. Then set aside 15 minutes to find at least one Bible verse or fact from this lesson that answers it.

### SUMMARY
- The most common reasons people give for believing the Bible — upbringing and personal experience — are not sufficient to answer a sincere skeptic.
- The Bible presents itself as historically reliable, eyewitness-based, prophetically fulfilled, and divinely inspired.
- Trusting the Bible is not blind faith; it is faith supported by verifiable evidence.

## Lesson 2 — A Reliable Collection of Historical Documents

### HOOK
Imagine you walk into a courtroom, and the only evidence the prosecution offers is a single document written by one person who claims to have seen something. How credible is that case? Now imagine forty different witnesses, writing in three different languages, from three different continents, spanning fifteen centuries, all telling the same story. That changes everything.

### CORE TEACHING
The Bible is not one book. It is a library of 66 books, written by more than 40 authors over roughly 1,500 years. These authors came from every walk of life: Moses was a prince-turned-shepherd, David was a shepherd-turned-king, Amos was a fig farmer, Luke was a physician, Peter was a fisherman, Paul was a trained religious scholar, Matthew was a tax collector. They wrote in three languages — Hebrew, Greek, and Aramaic — on three continents — Asia, Africa, and Europe. Most of them never met one another. Some lived centuries apart. Yet the books they produced fit together into one coherent story: God's plan to redeem a fallen world through Jesus Christ.

This is why the Bible is called a "canon" — a word that means a measuring rod or standard. The church did not invent the canon; it recognized which books had the unmistakable ring of divine authority. These are the books that the people of God consistently received as God-breathed.

Consider how we test any historical document. We ask: Is it internally consistent? Does it contradict itself? Are there multiple independent witnesses? Is it corroborated by external evidence? On every count, the Bible stands up to scrutiny. Over 25,000 archaeological digs have touched on the biblical record. Not one has disproven it. Many have confirmed details that skeptics once dismissed as legend — the existence of King David, the Hittite empire, the pool of Siloam, the governor Pontius Pilate.

To be clear: archaeology does not prove the Bible is God's Word. But it proves the Bible is reliable as a historical document. And if the Bible is reliable in the places we can test, it is reasonable to trust it in the places we cannot. This is the same standard we apply to any ancient document. The difference is that the Bible passes that standard far better than any other book from the ancient world.

### KEY SCRIPTURES
- 2 Peter 1:16 — "For we did not follow cunningly devised fables when we made known to you the power and coming of our Lord Jesus Christ, but were eyewitnesses of His majesty." (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- 1 Corinthians 15:3-8 — Paul recounts the resurrection and lists specific eyewitnesses, including over 500 people at once, "of whom the greater part remain to the present." (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]

### PRACTICAL APPLICATION
This week, pick one story from the Bible that skeptics often challenge — the existence of a specific people group, city, or ruler mentioned in Scripture. Use a Bible app or website to look up what archaeological evidence exists for it. Write down one finding in your phone notes.

### SUMMARY
- The Bible is 66 books written by 40+ authors over 1,500 years in three languages on three continents.
- Over 25,000 archaeological digs have corroborated the biblical record without a single disproving find.
- If the Bible is reliable in the places we can verify, it is reasonable to trust it in the places we cannot.

## Lesson 3 — Written by Eyewitnesses

### HOOK
In any courtroom, the strongest testimony comes from someone who saw the event with their own eyes. Secondhand testimony is weaker. Hearsay is often thrown out entirely. The writers of the Bible understood this. That is why they insisted — over and over — that they were not passing along rumors.

### CORE TEACHING
The apostle Peter, writing near the end of his life, was direct about this: "We did not follow cunningly devised fables when we made known to you the power and coming of our Lord Jesus Christ, but were eyewitnesses of His majesty" (2 Peter 1:16). Peter was not writing a religious opinion piece. He was giving eyewitness testimony.

Think about what this means. When Peter wrote about Jesus walking on water, he was there. When he wrote about the transfiguration, he saw it. When he wrote about the resurrection, he had seen the empty tomb and the risen Christ. The same is true of John, who wrote, "That which was from the beginning, which we have heard, which we have seen with our eyes, which we have looked upon, and our hands have handled, concerning the Word of life" (1 John 1:1). John does not say, "This is what we believe." He says, "This is what we touched."

Even Luke, who was not an eyewitness himself, made clear that he carefully investigated the accounts of those who were. He says he "followed all things closely for some time past" and wrote an orderly account so that his reader "may have certainty" (Luke 1:3-4). He did not ask for blind faith. He presented a case.

Consider what these people gave up for their testimony. Nearly every apostle died a martyr's death. People die for what they believe is true — that happens every day. But people do not die for what they know is a lie. Peter, James, Paul, and the others had every opportunity to recant and save their lives. They did not. Why? Because they had seen the risen Christ. You do not let yourself be crucified for a story you made up.

This does not prove the Bible is God's Word all by itself. But it establishes something critical: the New Testament is not mythology that developed over centuries. It is firsthand testimony written by people who were there.

### KEY SCRIPTURES
- 2 Peter 1:16 — "For we did not follow cunningly devised fables when we made known to you the power and coming of our Lord Jesus Christ, but were eyewitnesses of His majesty." (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- 1 John 1:1-3 — "That which was from the beginning, which we have heard, which we have seen with our eyes, which we have looked upon, and our hands have handled, concerning the Word of life... we proclaim to you." (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Luke 1:1-4 — Luke explains he investigated everything carefully from the beginning to write an orderly account. (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]

### PRACTICAL APPLICATION
This week, read one gospel — Mark is the shortest and can be read in about an hour. As you read, write down every place where the writer describes something as if he was there: what he saw, heard, or touched. At the end, ask yourself: does this read like a legend, or does it read like a witness giving testimony?

### SUMMARY
- The biblical writers repeatedly insist they were eyewitnesses, not creators of myths.
- Luke, a non-eyewitness historian, carefully investigated and verified the eyewitness accounts.
- The apostles' willingness to die for their testimony supports the truth of what they claimed.

## Lesson 4 — The Bible Is Falsifiable

### HOOK
A claim that cannot be tested is a claim you can only accept on blind faith. But what if a claim could be tested — and it passed? That is exactly what we find with the resurrection of Jesus. Paul did not ask his readers to take his word for it. He named names.

### CORE TEACHING
When the apostle Paul wrote to the church in Corinth around 55 AD, he summarized the gospel message and then did something remarkable. He listed the eyewitnesses to the resurrection: "He was seen by Cephas, then by the twelve. After that He was seen by over five hundred brethren at once, of whom the greater part remain to the present, but some have fallen asleep" (1 Corinthians 15:5-6). Why did Paul mention that most of the 500 were still alive? Because he was effectively saying, "Go ask them."

This is called falsifiability. A claim is falsifiable if it can be proven false. If Paul had made up the resurrection, all anyone had to do was interview one of the 500 witnesses and find out the truth. The fact that the early church grew in the very city where Jesus was crucified — Jerusalem — is evidence that the resurrection claim held up under scrutiny. If the body had still been in the tomb, the authorities would have produced it and ended the movement instantly. They did not, because they could not.

The same principle applies to the Bible as a whole. Many events recorded in Scripture were public events, witnessed by thousands. Jesus taught openly in the temple courts. He performed miracles in front of crowds. He was crucified publicly under Roman authority. This is not mythology that developed in a corner. It is public history.

Think about it this way. If someone told you they saw a miracle happen in a remote village with no other witnesses, you would be right to be skeptical. But if someone told you they saw a miracle happen in a crowded marketplace at noon, with hundreds of people watching, and they named witnesses you could go and interview — that is a different kind of claim. That is the kind of claim the Bible makes about the resurrection.

### KEY SCRIPTURES
- 1 Corinthians 15:3-8 — Paul lists eyewitnesses to the resurrection, including over 500 at once, most of whom were still alive when he wrote. (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Acts 2:22-24 — Peter preaches openly in Jerusalem that Jesus was "a Man attested by God to you by miracles, wonders, and signs" — publicly, not in secret. (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Acts 26:26 — Paul tells King Agrippa that these events "were not done in a corner" — they were public knowledge. (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]

### PRACTICAL APPLICATION
This week, write down one claim that a skeptic has made against the Bible. Then find the biblical passage that directly addresses that claim. Read it in context. If the Bible can be tested on that point, look up what the evidence actually says. Write down what you find.

### SUMMARY
- Paul names over 500 eyewitnesses to the resurrection and says most are still alive — making the claim testable.
- The early church grew in Jerusalem, where the crucifixion happened, proving the resurrection claim withstood scrutiny.
- The Bible records public events that were witnessed by thousands, not private myths that developed in secret.

## Lesson 5 — The Bible and the Supernatural

### HOOK
If the Bible were only a reliable history book, we could respect it the way we respect any ancient record. But the Bible does not stop at history. It reports events that no natural explanation can account for — and that is exactly where the story gets interesting.

### CORE TEACHING
Peter, after establishing that he was an eyewitness to Jesus, goes on to describe what he saw on the mount of transfiguration. Jesus was transformed before his eyes. Moses and Elijah — men who had died centuries earlier — appeared and spoke with Him. A voice from heaven declared, "This is My beloved Son, in whom I am well pleased" (2 Peter 1:17-18). Peter is reporting a supernatural event, not because he was naive, but because it actually happened.

The Bible is full of such reports. The Red Sea parts. A widow's son is raised from the dead. A fiery furnace does not burn three young men. A lion's mouth is shut. Blind people see. Deaf people hear. A dead man named Lazarus walks out of a tomb. And at the center of it all, Jesus Christ — crucified on Friday, buried on Saturday, risen on Sunday.

This is where many people stop. They can accept the Bible as history, but miracles make them uncomfortable. That discomfort usually comes from a hidden assumption: that the supernatural cannot happen because nature is all that exists. But that assumption is a philosophical belief, not a scientific fact. If there is a God who created the natural world, He is not bound by the rules of the world He made. A God who can create the universe can certainly part a sea, heal a blind man, or raise His Son from the dead.

The supernatural events in the Bible are not random stories scattered throughout the text. They cluster around the key moments of God's redemptive plan — the exodus, the ministry of Elijah and Elisha, the incarnation and ministry of Jesus, the birth of the church. They serve as signs that point to something greater: God is at work to save His people.

### KEY SCRIPTURES
- 2 Peter 1:17-18 — Peter describes hearing the voice of God on the mount of transfiguration. (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- John 20:30-31 — "And truly Jesus did many other signs in the presence of His disciples, which are not written in this book; but these are written that you may believe that Jesus is the Christ, the Son of God." (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Hebrews 2:3-4 — The gospel was "confirmed to us by those who heard Him, God also bearing witness both with signs and wonders, with various miracles, and gifts of the Holy Spirit." (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]

### PRACTICAL APPLICATION
This week, choose one miracle from the gospels — Jesus healing a blind man, calming a storm, or raising someone from the dead. Read the account slowly, imagining you were there. Write down one sentence about what that moment would have felt like as an eyewitness.

### SUMMARY
- The Bible reports supernatural events because the God who created nature is not limited by it.
- Miracles in Scripture cluster around key moments in God's redemptive plan — they are not random.
- Skepticism about miracles is usually based on a philosophical assumption, not evidence.

## Lesson 6 — Fulfilled Prophecy

### HOOK
A man writes a poem describing his own execution in detail — his hands and feet pierced, his garments divided by lot, his tormentors mocking him. The only problem is that the method of execution he describes had not been invented yet. He wrote it a thousand years before it happened.

### CORE TEACHING
Psalm 22 was written by David approximately 1,000 years before Jesus was born. In David's day, the worst form of capital punishment was stoning. Crucifixion did not exist yet. Yet Psalm 22 describes crucifixion in unmistakable detail: "They pierced My hands and My feet" (Psalm 22:16). "They divide My garments among them, and for My clothing they cast lots" (Psalm 22:18). The psalm opens with the exact words Jesus cried out on the cross: "My God, My God, why have You forsaken Me?" (Psalm 22:1; Matthew 27:46).

This is not vague fortune-telling. This is specific prophecy with specific fulfillment. And Psalm 22 is just one example. Isaiah 53, written 700 years before Christ, describes a suffering servant who would be "pierced for our transgressions" and "crushed for our iniquities" (Isaiah 53:5). He would be "numbered with the transgressors" (Isaiah 53:12) and make "intercession for the transgressors" — exactly what Jesus did on the cross.

The Old Testament contains hundreds of specific prophecies about the Messiah: where He would be born (Micah 5:2), that He would be born of a virgin (Isaiah 7:14), that He would come from the tribe of Judah (Genesis 49:10), that He would enter Jerusalem on a donkey (Zechariah 9:9), that He would be betrayed for thirty pieces of silver (Zechariah 11:12-13), that not one of His bones would be broken (Psalm 34:20; John 19:33-36). Every single one was fulfilled in Jesus Christ.

The mathematical probability of one person fulfilling even a handful of these prophecies by chance is astronomically small. But Jesus fulfilled them all. This is not coincidence. This is the signature of God on His Word.

Peter puts it this way: "We have the prophetic word made more sure, to which you do well to pay attention as to a lamp shining in a dark place" (2 Peter 1:19). Fulfilled prophecy is like a light in the darkness — it does not force anyone to believe, but it gives honest seekers something to follow until the light of Christ dawns in their hearts.

### KEY SCRIPTURES
- Psalm 22:1, 14-18 — A detailed description of crucifixion written 1,000 years before crucifixion existed. (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Isaiah 53:4-6 — "He was wounded for our transgressions, He was bruised for our iniquities... and by His stripes we are healed." (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- 2 Peter 1:19 — "We have the prophetic word made more sure, to which you do well to pay attention as to a lamp shining in a dark place." (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]

### PRACTICAL APPLICATION
This week, open your Bible to Isaiah 53 and read the entire chapter. Then open to a gospel account of the crucifixion — Matthew 27 or Luke 23. Write down three specific details from Isaiah 53 that you see fulfilled in the crucifixion accounts.

### SUMMARY
- Psalm 22, written 1,000 years before Christ, describes crucifixion in precise detail before the method existed.
- Hundreds of specific Old Testament prophecies about the Messiah were fulfilled in Jesus Christ.
- Fulfilled prophecy is evidence that Scripture is not human in origin — it bears God's signature.

## Lesson 7 — The Bible Is God's Word

### HOOK
Hundreds of times in the Bible, the writers use a phrase that should stop us cold: "Thus says the Lord." They were not claiming to share their opinions about God. They were claiming to speak for God. If that claim is true, then this book carries an authority that no other book in human history can match.

### CORE TEACHING
The Bible does not present itself as humanity's search for God. It presents itself as God's revelation to humanity. From Genesis to Revelation, the pattern repeats: "The Lord spoke to Moses, saying," "The word of the Lord came to me," "Thus says the Lord God." These are not polite suggestions. They are declarations of divine authority.

Peter clarifies how this works: "No prophecy of Scripture is of any private interpretation, for prophecy never came by the will of man, but holy men of God spoke as they were moved by the Holy Spirit" (2 Peter 1:20-21). The human authors were not taking dictation in a passive trance. They wrote from their own personalities, experiences, and language. Moses wrote like Moses. Paul wrote like Paul. John wrote like John. Yet the Holy Spirit so superintended their writing that the final product is exactly what God intended — His Word in human words.

Paul makes the same point: "All Scripture is given by inspiration of God" (2 Timothy 3:16). The word "inspiration" literally means "God-breathed." Scripture is not merely inspired in the way a poet is inspired. It is breathed out by God Himself. That is why it carries authority. That is why it is "profitable for doctrine, for reproof, for correction, for instruction in righteousness" (2 Timothy 3:16). It is not a textbook or a self-help manual. It is the living Word of the living God.

This has practical implications. If the Bible is God's Word, then we do not have the right to pick and choose which parts we obey. We do not get to accept the parts we like and ignore the parts that make us uncomfortable. The Bible is not a buffet. It is a letter from the King, and our job is to receive it, believe it, and obey it.

Jesus Himself set the example. When He was tempted by Satan in the wilderness, He answered every temptation with "It is written" (Matthew 4:4, 7, 10). He submitted His own will to the written Word. If the Son of God needed Scripture to resist temptation, how much more do we?

### KEY SCRIPTURES
- 2 Peter 1:20-21 — "No prophecy of Scripture is of any private interpretation... but holy men of God spoke as they were moved by the Holy Spirit." (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- 2 Timothy 3:16-17 — "All Scripture is given by inspiration of God, and is profitable for doctrine, for reproof, for correction, for instruction in righteousness." (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]
- Matthew 4:4 — Jesus answers Satan: "It is written, 'Man shall not live by bread alone, but by every word that proceeds from the mouth of God.'" (NKJV) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV]

### PRACTICAL APPLICATION
This week, choose one command in Scripture that you have been reluctant to obey. It could be forgiving someone, giving generously, speaking the truth, or setting aside time for prayer. Write the command on a note card and place it where you will see it every day. Commit to doing it before the week ends.

### SUMMARY
- The Bible claims to be God-breathed, not merely human religious writing.
- Human authors wrote in their own voices but were carried along by the Holy Spirit so that the final product is God's Word.
- If the Bible is God's Word, we are called to obey it — not pick and choose from it.
