# Study Guide: The Authority of Scripture

## 1. Core Definition

The authority of Scripture means the Bible is the final, sufficient, and infallible source of truth for what we believe and how we live. It is not merely a human book about God — it is God's own words to humanity, given through human authors who were carried along by the Holy Spirit. The Bible does not derive its authority from the church, from tradition, or from human reason. It carries authority because it is God-breathed (2 Timothy 3:16). To trust the Bible is to trust the God who spoke it.

## 2. Key Biblical Truths

- **Scripture Is God-Breathed** — 2 Timothy 3:16 declares that all Scripture is inspired by God, meaning it originates from Him. This is not dictation as much as divine superintendence: human authors wrote from their own experiences, languages, and styles, yet the Holy Spirit so guided them that what they wrote is exactly what God intended (2 Peter 1:20-21).

- **Scripture Is a Reliable Historical Record** — The Bible is a collection of 66 books, written by over 40 authors across 1,500 years on three continents (Asia, Africa, Europe) in three languages (Hebrew, Greek, Aramaic). Despite this diversity, it tells one unified redemptive story. Over 25,000 archaeological digs have corroborated the biblical record without a single disproving find [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV].

- **Scripture Is Eyewitness Testimony** — Peter writes, "we did not follow cleverly devised myths when we made known to you the power and coming of our Lord Jesus Christ, but we were eyewitnesses of His majesty" (2 Peter 1:16). Luke investigated everything carefully from the beginning and wrote an orderly account so that readers "may know the certainty of those things in which you were instructed" (Luke 1:1-4). John writes about "that which we have heard, which we have seen with our eyes, which we have looked upon and our hands have handled" (1 John 1:1) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV].

- **Scripture Is Falsifiable** — When Paul wrote about the resurrection in 1 Corinthians 15, he noted that Jesus appeared to over 500 people at once, "most of whom remain to the present" (1 Corinthians 15:6). This means the claim was testable — anyone could go and ask those witnesses. The gospel was never a private vision; it was public, verifiable history [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV].

- **Scripture Contains Fulfilled Prophecy** — Specific prophecies written hundreds or even thousands of years beforehand were fulfilled in precise detail. Psalm 22, written by David approximately 1,000 years before Christ, describes crucifixion in vivid detail — long before crucifixion was invented. Isaiah 53, written 700 years before Christ, describes the suffering servant who would be "pierced for our transgressions" [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV].

- **Scripture Functions as Final Authority** — Jesus Himself appealed to Scripture as the final court of appeal: "It is written" (Matthew 4:4, 7, 10). The early church tested everything by Scripture (Acts 17:11). Scripture is profitable for "teaching, for reproof, for correction, for training in righteousness, that the man of God may be complete, equipped for every good work" (2 Timothy 3:16-17) [VERIFY AGAINST NKJV].

## 3. Common Misconceptions

- **"I believe the Bible because I was raised that way."** — This is not a reasoned answer. People are raised in many religions. How you were raised does not determine what is true.

- **"I believe the Bible because I tried it and it worked for me."** — Experience is not a reliable test of truth. People try all kinds of things and have their lives changed — including false religions. Personal experience confirms truth but does not establish it.

- **"The Bible has been translated so many times it cannot be trusted."** — This misunderstands how translation works. Modern translations do not translate from earlier translations; they go back directly to the original Hebrew, Greek, and Aramaic manuscripts. The "telephone game" analogy does not apply.

- **"We don't have the original manuscripts, so we cannot know what the Bible originally said."** — While we do not have the autographs, we have over 6,000 Greek New Testament manuscripts — far more than any other ancient document. The earliest fragments date to within decades of the original writings. By comparison, Caesar's Gallic Wars has 10 manuscripts, the earliest 900 years after the original, and no one questions its authenticity.

- **"Overzealous monks changed the Bible to fit church teaching."** — This conspiracy theory requires monks to have changed over 6,000 Greek manuscripts, all early translations (Syriac, Coptic, Latin), and all the writings of the early church fathers — in multiple languages — without anyone noticing or leaving evidence. This is historically impossible.

- **"You cannot use the Bible to prove the Bible — that is circular reasoning."** — When someone asks why you believe the Bible, you are not trying to prove it is true by appealing to itself. You are explaining your reasons for trusting it. The answer is based on the historical evidence for the Bible's reliability — eyewitness testimony, manuscript evidence, archaeological corroboration, fulfilled prophecy — not a logical circle.

- **"The scientific method cannot verify the Bible, so it is not trustworthy."** — The scientific method applies to observable, measurable, repeatable phenomena. Historical documents cannot be tested that way. History is investigated through the evidentiary method — the same method used in courtrooms — which examines witness testimony, corroboration, internal consistency, and external evidence.

## 4. Practical Application Angles

- **Equip believers to give a reasoned answer** — Many Christians know what they believe but not why. Learning the evidence for Scripture's reliability prepares them to answer skeptics with confidence, not defensiveness.

- **Build confidence in daily Bible reading** — When believers know their Bible is trustworthy, they read it with greater expectation and obedience. Doubt about the Bible's reliability undermines the very foundation of faith.

- **Prepare for skeptical challenges** — College campuses, workplaces, and online spaces regularly challenge the Bible's authority. Christians need to know how to respond to common objections without being intimidated.

- **Deepen worship through fulfilled prophecy** — Seeing how God's Word foretold events centuries in advance strengthens awe and trust in a God who governs history.

- **Strengthen the local church** — When church members understand why Scripture is authoritative, they are less likely to be tossed to and fro by every new teaching and more likely to ground their lives in the Word.

## 5. Key Terms & Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Inspiration | The work of the Holy Spirit by which He guided the human authors of Scripture so that what they wrote was the very Word of God (2 Timothy 3:16, 2 Peter 1:21) |
| Inerrancy | The doctrine that Scripture, in its original manuscripts, is without error in all that it affirms |
| Authority of Scripture | The Bible's right to rule the belief and practice of every believer, because it is God's Word |
| Sufficiency of Scripture | The truth that Scripture contains everything necessary for salvation and godly living (2 Timothy 3:15-17) |
| Canon of Scripture | The collection of books that the church recognized as divinely inspired — 66 books in the Protestant canon |
| Autographs | The original manuscripts of the biblical books, none of which survive today |
| Textual Criticism | The scholarly discipline of comparing existing manuscripts to determine the most likely original reading |
| Falsifiability | The quality of a claim that makes it possible to be proven false — the resurrection accounts are falsifiable because Paul names living eyewitnesses who could be questioned |
| Expository Apologetics | The practice of defending the faith by explaining what Scripture says in its context, letting the Bible answer objections rather than relying on human reasoning alone |
| Evidentiary Method | The method of investigating historical claims by examining eyewitness testimony, corroboration, internal consistency, and external evidence — as opposed to the scientific method which requires observation and repetition |
