# Study Guide: The Cross & Resurrection

## Module Overview
Module 06 — The Cross & Resurrection examines the meaning of Christ's atoning death and victorious resurrection: the divine dilemma of how God can be just and justify sinners, substitutionary atonement, propitiation, justification, the resurrection as historical fact and theological necessity, and the hope of the resurrection body.

---

## 1. The Divine Dilemma

### The Problem
Exodus 34:6-7 presents one of the greatest revelations of God in Scripture. God proclaims Himself as "compassionate and gracious, slow to anger, and abounding in lovingkindness and truth; who keeps lovingkindness for thousands, who forgives iniquity, transgression and sin." But the same verse declares: "yet He will by no means leave the guilty unpunished."

Here is the central problem of the Bible: **How can God forgive all types and kinds of sin while also punishing every type and kind of sin?**

### Proverbs 17:15
"He who justifies the wicked and he who condemns the righteous, both of them alike are an abomination to the Lord." A judge who declares a guilty person righteous is corrupt. Yet the New Testament teaches that God justifies the wicked (sinners who believe in Christ). How can God do what He Himself calls an abomination?

### The Answer: The Cross
The entire Bible moves toward the answer at Calvary. God can be just and the justifier of those who believe in Jesus because Christ bore the punishment that our sins deserved. God did not sweep sin under the rug — He punished every sin of His people in His Son on the cross.

---

## 2. The Cup of Wrath

### Gethsemane
In the Garden of Gethsemane, Jesus prayed three times: "My Father, if it is possible, let this cup pass from Me; yet not as I will, but as You will" (Matthew 26:39). What was in this cup that caused the Son of God to be in such agony that His sweat became like drops of blood?

### Not Fear of Physical Suffering
Church history records that thousands of Christians went to their crosses singing hymns and rejoicing. If mere disciples could face crucifixion with joy, the Captain of their salvation was not cowering in a garden over nails and a spear. The cup was something far worse.

### The Wrath of God
The cup is a biblical symbol for the wrath of God. Jeremiah 25:15-16: "Take this cup of the wine of wrath from My hand and cause all the nations to whom I send you to drink it." What Jesus faced in Gethsemane was the full, undiluted wrath of Almighty God against all the sins of His people. This was the holy hatred of God against evil — not against evil in the abstract, but against the specific sins of every person who would believe.

### The Three Hours of Darkness
From the sixth hour to the ninth hour, darkness fell over the whole land (Matthew 27:45). Jesus was shut up in a room of pitch blackness as wave after wave of God's wrath poured over Him. In that darkness, He cried out: "My God, My God, why have You forsaken Me?" (Matthew 27:46). This was not a theatrical cry — it was the Son experiencing the separation from the Father that our sins deserved.

### Why Forsaken?
Psalm 22 answers this question. The Psalmist notes that God has always answered those who cried out to Him — Abraham, Moses, David. But when the Sinless One cried out, there was only silence. Why? "But I am a worm and not a man" (Psalm 22:6). Christ was bearing the sins of His people, and a holy God turned away from Him as He must turn away from every sinner. The answer to "why have You forsaken Me" is: because You are holy, and I am bearing sin.

## 3. Substitutionary Atonement

### Christ Died in Our Place
The heart of the gospel is substitution: Christ died in the place of sinners. "He made Him who knew no sin to be sin on our behalf, so that we might become the righteousness of God in Him" (2 Corinthians 5:21). Christ was not merely an example or a martyr — He was a substitute who bore the penalty we deserved.

### Imputation
Imputation means that a legal transfer took place at the cross. Our sin was imputed (credited) to Christ, and His righteousness is imputed (credited) to us. He was treated as guilty so that we could be treated as righteous. "The LORD has caused the iniquity of us all to fall upon Him" (Isaiah 53:6).

### Becoming a Curse
"Christ redeemed us from the curse of the Law, having become a curse for us" (Galatians 3:13). Deuteronomy 27 lists the curses that fall on the disobedient — curses for idolatry, dishonoring parents, murder, immorality, and every violation of God's law. On the cross, Christ was cursed as an idolator, cursed as a murderer, cursed as an adulterer — bearing every curse that belonged to His people.

### The Value of Christ's Sacrifice
A student once asked Paul Washer: "How can one man suffering a few short hours on a tree save a multitude from an eternity in hell?" The answer is the infinite worth of Christ. Put everything that exists — every mountain, every star, every galaxy — on one side of the scale, and put Jesus on the other. He outweighs them all. Because the One who died was God in the flesh, His sacrifice has infinite value.

### Propitiation
Romans 3:25 declares that God displayed Christ publicly as a propitiation (hilasterion) in His blood. Propitiation is a sacrifice that satisfies the justice of God and appeases His wrath. The cross does not merely demonstrate God's love — it satisfies His justice. God did not set aside His wrath; He poured it out on His Son.

## 4. Justification

### A Legal Declaration
Justification is not God making us righteous by an internal change — it is God declaring us righteous as a legal verdict. "Being justified as a gift by His grace through the redemption which is in Christ Jesus" (Romans 3:24). It is a forensic declaration from the throne of God.

### By Grace Alone
Justification is a gift. It cannot be earned. There are only two types of religion in the world: religion of works (human merit) and religion of grace (unearned favor). All religions except Christianity are systems of works — the adherent earns salvation through his own righteousness. But the Christian confesses: "I will go to heaven based upon the virtue and merit of another, Jesus Christ my Lord."

### God Is Both Just and Justifier
The cross enables God to be both just and the justifier of the one who has faith in Jesus (Romans 3:26). God's justice was fully satisfied at Calvary. Christ said "It is finished" — the debt is paid in full. This is not a pardon that leaves the crime outstanding — this is a payment that fully satisfies the demands of justice.

### The Guarantee of the Resurrection
Christ was "delivered over because of our transgressions, and was raised because of our justification" (Romans 4:25). The resurrection is God's public declaration that the sacrifice of Christ was accepted. When God raised Jesus from the dead, He was saying: "The payment is complete. Justice is satisfied. My Son has accomplished what He set out to do."

## 5. The Resurrection: Historical Fact

### The Capstone of Christianity
Christianity rises and falls with the resurrection of Jesus Christ. Paul does not treat the resurrection as an optional belief — it is a matter of "first importance" (1 Corinthians 15:3). If there is no resurrection, there is no Christianity.

### The Argument from Authority
Paul begins 1 Corinthians 15 by reminding the Corinthians of the gospel he preached to them: "that Christ died for our sins according to the Scriptures, and that He was buried, and that He was raised on the third day according to the Scriptures" (1 Corinthians 15:3-4). This is the apostolic gospel. Those who deny the resurrection are not offering an alternative interpretation — they are denying Christianity itself. As Jesus told Peter who offered a different interpretation of His mission: "Get behind Me, Satan" (Mark 8:33).

### The Argument from Evidence
Paul lists multiple eyewitnesses: Peter, the Twelve, more than 500 brothers at one time (most still alive when Paul wrote), James, all the apostles, and Paul himself (1 Corinthians 15:5-8). This is the most well-attested fact in ancient history. When 1 Corinthians was written, at least 300 eyewitnesses to the resurrection were still alive and could be questioned.

### The Argument from Logic
Paul argues that if there is no resurrection, seven devastating conclusions follow:
1. Christ has not been raised
2. Preaching is useless
3. Faith is futile
4. The apostles are liars misrepresenting God
5. Believers are still in their sins
6. The dead in Christ have perished
7. Christians are the most pitied people in the world

But since Christ has been raised, the opposite is true: preaching has power, faith is real, God is truthful, sins are forgiven, the dead will rise, and Christians have genuine hope.

### Refuting the Hallucination Theory
Skeptics sometimes argue that the disciples hallucinated the resurrection. This theory fails on multiple grounds: 500 people do not share the same hallucination; hallucinations do not appear to groups at different times and places; and if the disciples hallucinated, the body would still have been in the tomb. The tomb was empty.

## 6. The Resurrection Body

### A New Kind of Body
Paul addresses the question: "How are the dead raised? With what kind of body do they come?" (1 Corinthians 15:35). The resurrection body is not a resuscitation of the old body — it is a transformation. The body is sown perishable and raised imperishable; sown in dishonor and raised in glory; sown in weakness and raised in power; sown a natural body and raised a spiritual body (1 Corinthians 15:42-44).

### The Last Adam
The first Adam became a living being; the Last Adam (Christ) became a life-giving spirit (1 Corinthians 15:45). As we have borne the image of the man of dust (Adam), we shall also bear the image of the man of heaven (Christ). Our resurrection is secured by our union with Christ. As our federal head, Christ's resurrection guarantees our resurrection.

### A Mystery Revealed
"We will not all sleep, but we will all be changed, in a moment, in the twinkling of an eye, at the last trumpet" (1 Corinthians 15:51-52). The perishable must put on the imperishable; the mortal must put on immortality. This is the hope of every believer — not escape from the body, but the redemption of the body.

### The Pattern Son
Christ is the firstborn from the dead (Colossians 1:18). He is the prototype of the new creation. The resurrected Christ is a species that never existed before — a man who can never die again. And those who are in Christ will share in that same resurrection life.

---

## 7. Victory Over Death

### Death Is Swallowed Up
When the perishable puts on the imperishable and the mortal puts on immortality, then the prophecy will be fulfilled: "Death is swallowed up in victory. O death, where is your victory? O death, where is your sting?" (1 Corinthians 15:54-55).

### The Sting Removed
The sting of death is sin, and the power of sin is the law. But Christ has dealt with both. He satisfied the law's demands through His perfect obedience, and He bore the penalty for sin through His substitutionary death. The sting has been removed.

### Resurrection Life Now
The resurrection is not only a future hope — it is a present reality. We have been raised with Christ and made to sit with Him in heavenly places (Ephesians 2:5-6). We are to walk in newness of life (Romans 6:4). The same power that raised Christ from the dead is at work in us. This resurrection life has absolute mastery over sin, death, and Satan.

### Therefore
Because the resurrection is true, Paul calls believers to be "steadfast, immovable, always abounding in the work of the Lord, knowing that your labor is not in vain in the Lord" (1 Corinthians 15:58). The resurrection changes our anthropology (how we see humanity), our soteriology (how we understand salvation), and our missiology (why we labor in the gospel). Our work matters because the resurrection is real.

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Propitiation | A sacrifice that satisfies God's wrath and justice (Romans 3:25) |
| Substitutionary Atonement | Christ died in the place of sinners, bearing the penalty they deserved |
| Imputation | The legal transfer of our sin to Christ and His righteousness to us |
| Justification | God's legal declaration that a believer is righteous through faith in Christ |
| Divine Dilemma | The problem of how God can be just and also justify the wicked |
| Federal Headship | Adam represented all humanity in the fall; Christ represents all believers in redemption |
| Resurrection Body | The imperishable, glorified body believers will receive at Christ's return |
| New Creation | The new species of being that believers become through union with the resurrected Christ |
